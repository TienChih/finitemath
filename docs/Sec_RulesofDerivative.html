Section 7.4 Basic rules of the derivative

While there is a formal definition of a derivative, it is a somewhat cumbersome and computationally intense definition. In this section, we begin introducing some properties of the derivative which simplify computation.

Subsection 7.4.1 Basic Rules

Remark 7.4.1.

Given functions \(f(x), g(x)\) and constants \(a,b\) we have:

\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[a(f)x)+bg(x)]=af'(x)+bg'(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Where \(\frac{d}{dx}\) means “to take the dertivative with respect to \(x\)”.

How do we know that? Well:

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}[a(f)x)+bg(x)]\amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{af(x+h)+bg(x+h)-(af(x)-bg(x))}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{af(x+h)-af(x)}{h}+\limit{h}{0}\frac{bg(x+h)-bg(x)}{h}\\ \amp = a\limit{h}{0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}+b\limit{h}{0}\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}\\ \amp = af'(x)+bg'(x)\text{.} \end{align*}

Subsection 7.4.2 Power Rule

We may have noticed a pattern with derivatives. But to illustrate it explicitly:

  • The derivative of \(f(x)=x^0\text{,}\) it's the derivative of the constant function \(f(x)=1\text{,}\) and since its a horizontal line, it never has any slope and \(f'(x)=0\text{.}\)

  • The derivative of \(f(x)=x^1\text{,}\) it's the derivative of the line \(f(x)=x\) which always has slope 1, so \(f'(x)=1\text{.}\)

  • The derivative of \(f(x)=x^2\) is:

    \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^2]\amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{(x+h)^2-x^2}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{x^2+2xh+h^2-x^2}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{(2x+h)h}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 2x+h=2x\text{.} \end{align*}

  • The derivative of \(f(x)=x^3\) is:

    \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^3]\amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{(x+h)^3-x^3}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{x^3+3x^2h+3xh^2+h^3-x^3}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{(3x^2+3xh+h^2)h}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 3x^2+3xh+h^2=3x^2\text{.} \end{align*}

To generalize this, we have a rule of differentiation: power rule.

Remark 7.4.2.

\(\frac{d}{dx}[x^n]=nx^{n-1}\text{.}\)

To verify:

  • \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^0]=0x^{0-1}=0\text{.}\)

  • \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^1]=1x^{1-1}=1\text{.}\)

  • \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^2]=2x^{2-1}=2x\text{.}\)

  • \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^3]=3x^{3-1}=3x^2\text{.}\)

What's fascinating is that this rule applies to all powers, not just integer powers.

Remark 7.4.3.

By rewriting functions into their power rule form:

  • \(\frac{d}{dx}[\frac{1}{x^n}]=\frac{d}{dx}[x^{-n}]=-nx^{-n+1}=\frac{-n}{x^{-n+1}}\text{.}\)
  • \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sqrt[n]{x}]=\frac{d}{dx}[x^{1/n}]=-\frac{1}{n}x^{\frac{1-n}{n}}=\frac{1}{\sqrt[n]{x^{n-1}}}\text{.}\)
Example 7.4.4.

Find the derivative of \(f(x)=x^4-2x^2+7x-3\text{.}\)

Solution

By Remark 7.4.3 and Remark 7.4.1 we have:

\begin{align*} f'(x)\amp = \frac{d}{dx}[x^4-2x^2+7x-3]\\ \amp = \frac{d}{dx}[x^4-2x^2+7x^1-3x^0]\\ \amp = 4x^3-2(2x^1)+7(1x^0)-3(0x^{-1})\\ \amp = 4x^3-4x+7\text{.} \end{align*}

Example 7.4.5.

Find the derivative of \(g(x)=\sqrt{4x}+\frac{3}{x}-\frac{2}{x^2}\text{.}\)

Solution

By Remark 7.4.3 and Remark 7.4.1 we have:

\begin{align*} g'(x)\amp = \frac{d}{dx}[\sqrt{4x}+\frac{3}{x}-\frac{2}{x^2}]\\ \amp = \frac{d}{dx}[2x^{1/2}+3x^{-1}-2x^{-2}]\\ \amp = 2(1/2)x^{-1/2}+3(-1x^{-2})-2(2x^{-3})\\ \amp = x^{-1/2}-3x^{-2}+4x^{-3}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}}-\frac{3}{x^2}+\frac{4}{x^3}\text{.} \end{align*}

Checkpoint 7.4.6.

An analyst has found that a company’s costs and revenues in dollars for one product are given by

\begin{equation*} C(x)=2x, R(x)=6x-\frac{x^2}{100} \end{equation*}

respectively, where \(x\) is the number of items produced.

  1. Find the marginal cost function (\(R'(x)\) ).
  2. Find the marginal revenue function (\(C'(x)\) ).
  3. Using the fact that profit is the difference between revenue and costs, find the marginal profit function.
  4. What value of \(x\) makes marginal profit equal 0?
  5. Find the profit when the marginal profit is 0.
Solution

We have:

  1. \(C'(x)=\frac{d}{dx}[2x]=2\text{.}\) So \(\$2\) per product
  2. \(R'(x)=\frac{d}{dx}[6x-\frac{x^2}{100}]=6-\frac{x}{50}\text{.}\) So \(\$(6-\frac{x}{50})\) per product
  3. \(P(x)=R(x)-C(x)=6x-\frac{x^2}{100}-2x=4x-\frac{x^2}{100}.\text{.}\)
  4. Since \(P'(x)=\frac{d}{dx}[4x-\frac{x^2}{100}]=4-\frac{x}{50} \text{,}\) we note that this is zero when:
    \begin{align*} 4-\frac{x}{50}\amp =0\\ 4\amp= \frac{x}{50}\\ x\amp=200\text{.} \end{align*}
    So marginal profit is 0 when 200 units of the product are produced.
  5. We have \(P(200)=4(200)-\frac{200^2}{100} =\$400\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 7.4.7.

If the price in dollars of a product is given by

\begin{equation*} p(q)=\frac{500}{q^2}+800 \end{equation*}

where \(q\) represents the demand for the product, find the marginal revenue when the demand is 4.

Solution

We note that revenue is price times quantity demanded so \(R(q)=p(q)q=(\frac{500}{q^2}+800)q=\frac{500}{q}+800q.\) From here we can find the marginal revenue: \(R'(q)=\frac{d}{dq}[\frac{500}{q}+800q]=\frac{d}{dq}[500q^{-1}+800q]=-500q^{-2}+800=\frac{-500}{q^2}+800\text{.}\)

Thus, when demand is 4, the marginal revenue is \(R'(4)=\frac{-500}{4^2}+800=\$768.75\) per unit. We can see this below:

Checkpoint 7.4.8.

Insulin affects the glucose, or blood sugar, level of some diabetics according to the function

\begin{equation*} G(x)=-0.2x^2 +450, \end{equation*}

where \(G(x)\) is the blood sugar level 1 hour after \(x\) units of insulin are injected. (This mathematical model is only approximate, and it is valid only for values of x less than about 40.)

  1. Find the blood sugar level after 10 units of insulin are injected.
  2. Find the blood sugar level after 25 units of insulin are injected.
  3. Find the rate of change of blood sugar level after 10 units of insulin are injected.
  4. Find the rate of change of blood sugar level after 25 units of insulin are injected.
Solution

We note that \(G'(x)=\frac{d}{dx}[-0.2x^2 +450]=-0.4x \text{.}\)

  1. \(\displaystyle G(10)=-0.2(10)^2 +450=430.\)
  2. \(\displaystyle G(25)=-0.2(25)^2 +450=325.\)
  3. \(\displaystyle G'(10)=-0.4(10)=-4.\)
  4. \(\displaystyle G'(25)=-0.4(25) =-10.\)

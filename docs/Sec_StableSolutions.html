Section 11.2 Stable Solutions and Equilibrium

In this section, we discuss stable stable solutions to Markov systems. If I repeat a Markov process over and over, does it trend torwards some some of state where it won't change? Or does it go back and forth between multiple states in a cycle? Is there an initial starting state so that each iteration of the Markov process gives you back the exact same state? We examine these questions.

Subsection 11.2.1 Stable Solutions

Suppose you had two cities, \(X, Y\text{.}\) Each year 60% of city \(X\)'s population moved to \(Y\) and 20% of \(Y\)'s population moves to \(X\text{.}\) If the cities had 300000 people in total, how could we distribute these people so each year the cities populations remained the same?

What we are solving is essentionally \(\begin{pmatrix} 0.6 \amp 0.2 \\ 0.4 \amp 0.8\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} X_0 \\ Y_0 \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} X_0 \\ Y_0 \end{pmatrix}\) So recall Definition 2.3.5:

\begin{align*} \begin{pmatrix} 0.6 \amp 0.2 \\ 0.4 \amp 0.8\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} X_0 \\ Y_0 \end{pmatrix} \amp =\begin{pmatrix} X_0 \\ Y_0 \end{pmatrix} \\ \begin{pmatrix} 0.6 \amp 0.2 \\ 0.4 \amp 0.8\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} X_0 \\ Y_0 \end{pmatrix} \amp = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} X_0 \\ Y_0 \end{pmatrix} \\ \begin{pmatrix} 0.6 \amp 0.2 \\ 0.4 \amp 0.8\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} X_0 \\ Y_0 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} X_0 \\ Y_0 \end{pmatrix} \amp=\begin{pmatrix} 0\\0 \end{pmatrix}\\ \begin{pmatrix} -0.4 \amp 0.2 \\ 0.4 \amp -0.2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} X_0 \\ Y_0 \end{pmatrix} \amp=\begin{pmatrix} 0\\0 \end{pmatrix}\\ \begin{pmatrix} -0.4X_0 +0.2Y_0 \\ 0.4X_0 -0.2Y_0\end{pmatrix} \amp=\begin{pmatrix} 0\\0 \end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

We solve this system:

And we obtain:

\begin{align*} X_0-\frac{1}{2}Y_0\amp=0\\ 2X_0 \amp =Y_0. \end{align*}

So \(Y\)'s population would be double \(X\)'s. Since there are 300000 people, this would occur when \(X_0=100000, Y_0=200000\text{:}\)

Notice that the next year, since the populations remain the same, reapplying \(M\) continues to preserve the populations, nothing changes. Nor the year after and so on.

Remark 11.2.1.

In general, stable solutions to Markov chains are vectors \(\mathbf{x}\) such that \(M\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{x}\text{.}\) These can be found by solving:

\begin{equation*} (M-I_n)\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0}. \end{equation*}
Checkpoint 11.2.2.

Suppose you have a bizzare compound stored a chamber with different areas with different temperatures and pressures. Thus it is always changing between the 3 states of matter. After each hour, 20% of any solid becomes liquid and 20% of the solid becomes gas. 10% of liquids become solid and 30% becomes gas. 15% of gas becomes solid and 40% becomes liquid. What are initial amounts of solid liquid and gas so the amount of each never changes?

Solution

If solid, liquid, gas are states 1,2 and 3, then we have transition matrix:

\begin{equation*} M=\begin{pmatrix}0.6 \amp 0.1 \amp 0.15 \\ 0.2 \amp 0.6 \amp 0.4 \\ 0.2 \amp 0.3 \amp 0.45 \end{pmatrix} . \end{equation*}

Thus to find a stable solution, we solve:

\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix}-0.4 \amp 0.1 \amp 0.15 \\ 0.2 \amp -0.4 \amp 0.4 \\ 0.2 \amp 0.3 \amp -0.55 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} S \\L \\ G\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\\0 \end{pmatrix} . \end{equation*}

Doing so, we obtain:

\begin{align*} S\amp=\frac{5}{7}G\\ L\amp=\frac{19}{14}G \end{align*}

Which then, given any amount of gas, we could find how mnuch solid and liquid there were.

So if say we have 1000 kg of the compound, we could solve the system:

\begin{align*} S\amp=\frac{5}{7}G\\ L\amp=\frac{19}{14}G\\ S+L+G\amp=1000 \end{align*}

and obtain roughly 232.56kg of solid, 441.86kg of liquid and 325.58kg of gas.

Then say we have 500 g of the compound, we could solve the system:

\begin{align*} S\amp=\frac{5}{7}G\\ L\amp=\frac{19}{14}G\\ S+L+G\amp=500 \end{align*}

and obtain roughly 116.28g of solid, 220.93g of liquid and 162.79g of gas.

Definition 11.2.3.

Given a transition matrix \(M\) an equilibirum solution is a solution \(\mathbf{e}\) so that as \(i\) increases

\begin{equation*} M^i\mathbf{x}\to \mathbf{e}. \end{equation*}
Remark 11.2.4.

Equilibrium solutions are stable solutions.

What if \(Y\) had 300000 people and \(X\) was a ghost town with no one in it? We see that after 100 iterations:

We achieve the same state, which incidentally was the stable solution we found earlier!

Will this always happen? Consider the transition matrix

\begin{equation*} M=\begin{pmatrix} 0\amp 1 \ 1 \amp 0\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

It has stable solution when

\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} -1\amp 1 \ 1 \amp 0-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

which occurs when \(x=y\text{.}\) So if our initial state is \(x=5, y=5\text{,}\) in 100 iterations:

We see that we have stability. But what about if our initial state were \(x=10, y=0\text{?}\)
We see that we oscilate between states \(\begin{pmatrix} 10\\0\end{pmatrix}\) and \(\begin{pmatrix} 0\\10\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) We do not achieve equilibrium.

So when can we achieve equilibrium?

Definition 11.2.5.

A transition matrix is regular if each entry is positive (not zero).

Theorem 11.2.6.

If a transition matrix is regular, then it will achieve equilibrium.

Note that non-regular matrices may also achieve equilibrium. The above claim merely says, every regular matrix is gaurunteed to do so.

Checkpoint 11.2.7.

Tech companies Axiom, Belafonte and Cybertech hire and rehire from the same talent pool. Each month, 20% of Axiom's workers leave for Belafonte and 15% leave for Cybertech. 10% of Belafonte's go to Axiom and 25% go to Cybertech, and 30% of Cybertech's workers leave for Axiom, 10% for Belafonte. Over time, what proportion of the workforce will each company employ?

Solution

Note we have transition matrix

\begin{equation*} M=\begin{pmatrix} 0.65 \amp 0.1 \amp 0.3 \\ 0.2 \amp 0.65 \amp 0.1 \\ 0.15 \amp 0.25 \amp 0.6 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}

Since this is regular, we may just solve for a stable solution:

\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} -0.35 \amp 0.1 \amp 0.3 \\ 0.2 \amp -0.35 \amp 0.1 \\ 0.15 \amp 0.25 \amp -0.4 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} a\\b\\c\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\\0\end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}

Doing so gives us:

\begin{align*} a\amp =\frac{46}{41}c\\ b\amp =\frac{38}{41}c \end{align*}

So if we solve:

\begin{align*} a\amp =\frac{46}{41}c\\ b\amp =\frac{38}{41}c\\ a+b+c\amp=1 \end{align*}

We get \(a=\frac{46}{125}, b=\frac{38}{125}, c=\frac{41}{125}\) each a fraction of the total workforce.

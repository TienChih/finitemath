Section 9.3 Applications to Probability

In this section, we apply together what's learned from Chapter 8 and Section 9.1, Section 9.2 to compute probabilities.

Subsection 9.3.1 Turning Counting into Probability

Recall Definition 8.2.10. We are now equipped to find the cardinality of both \(A\)and \(S\text{.}\)

Example 9.3.1.

Consider a 52 card deck, and draw 5 cards without replacement.

  1. What is the probability that all 5 cards are spades?
  2. What is probability that all 5 cards are the same suit?
  3. What is the probability that are exactly two heart?
Solution

Recall that there are \(C(52,5)\) ways to select 5 cards of 52. Thus \(|S|=C(52,5)=2598960\text{.}\)

  1. There are 13 spades so the number of ways to select 5 spades is \(C(13,5)=1287\text{.}\) Thus the probability of selecting 5 cards and getting all spades is \(\frac{C(13,5)}{C(52,5)}=\frac{1287}{2598960}\approx 0.00495\text{.}\)
  2. The number of ways to select all cards from the same suit is still \(C(13,5)\) but since there are 4 suits, the probability of selecting 5 cards and they are all the same suit is \(\frac{4\cdot C(13,5)}{C(52,5)}=\frac{4\cdot 1287}{2598960}\approx 0.0198107\text{.}\)
  3. We select 2 out of 13 hearts and 3 out of 39 non-hearts, and there are \(C(13,2)\cdot C(39,3)=78\cdot 9139=712842\) ways to do this. Thus the probability you pick 5 cards and exactly 2 are hearts is \(\frac{C(13,2)\cdot C(39,3)}{C(52,5)}=\frac{712842}{259860}\approx 0.27428\)
Example 9.3.2.

Consider a 4 digit passcode consisting of digits 0 through 9.

  1. What is the probability that a random passcode doesn't repeat digits?
  2. What is probability that a random passcode only uses even digits?
  3. What is the probability only uses even digits without repitition?
Solution

Recall that there are \(10\cdot10\cdot10\cdot10=10^4=1000\) possible passcodes. Thus \(|S|=10000\text{.}\)

  1. There are \(P(10,4)=10\cdot9\cdot8\cdot7=5040\) passcodes that don't repeat digits. Thus the probability that a passcode doesn't repeat digits is \(\frac{P(10,4)}{10^4}=\frac{5040}{10000}=0.504\) .
  2. There are \(5^4=625\) passcodes that only use even digits. Thus the probability that a passcode uses only even digits is \(\frac{5^4}{10^4}=\frac{625}{10000}=0.0625\) .
  3. There are \(P(5,4)=5\cdot4\cdot3\cdot2=120\) passcodes that don't repeat digits and only use even digits. Thus the probability that a passcode doesn't repeat digits and only uses even digits is \(\frac{P(5,4)}{10^4}=\frac{120}{10000}=0.012\) .

Subsection 9.3.2 Operations on Events and Counting

We can also apply all the probability techniques from Chapter 8 to these situations.

Example 9.3.3.

Consider a 52 card deck, and draw 5 cards without replacement.

  1. What is the probability that is at least one diamond?
  2. What the cards are all back or all non-face cards (2-9)?
  3. If the cards are all black, what is the probability they are all non-face cards?
Solution

Consider:

  1. If \(D\) is the event “at least one diamond”, then \(D^c\) means “no diamonds”. \(P(D^c)=\frac{C(39,5)}{C(52,5)}=\frac{575757}{2598960}\approx 0.2215336\text{,}\) since there are 29 non diamonds to pick 5 from. So \(P(D)=1-P(D^c)=1-\frac{C(39,5)}{C(52,5)}\approx 0.7784664.\)
  2. Let \(B\) denote “all black cards”, and \(N\) denote “all non-face cards”. Note \(P(B)=\frac{C(26,5)}{C(52,5)}\text{,}\) \(P(N)=\frac{C(36,5)}{C(52,5)}\text{,}\) and \(P(B\cap N)=\frac{C(18,5)}{C(52,5)}\text{.}\) Thus
    \begin{equation*} P(B\cup N)=P(B)+P(N)-P(B\cap N)=\frac{C(26,5)+C(36,5)-C(18,5)}{C(52,5)}=\frac{65780+376992-8568}{2598960}\approx 0.167068. \end{equation*}
  3. This is \(P(N|B)=\frac{P(N\cap B)}{P(B)}=\frac{C(18,5)/C(52,5)}{C(26,5)/C(52,5)}=\frac{C(18,5)}{C(26,5)}=\frac{8568}{65780}\approx 0.130252.\)
Example 9.3.4.

Consider a 4 digit passcode consisting of digits 0 through 9.

  1. What is the probability that a random passcode doesn't repeat digits or the digits are all even?
  2. If the digits are all even, what's the probability that they don't repeat?
  3. Are digits repeating and digits being even independent?
Solution

Let \(R\) denote “digits don't repeat” and \(E\) denote “digits are all even.”:

  1. Recall Example 9.3.2, we say \(P(R)=\frac{P(10,4)}{10^4}, P(E)=\frac{5^4}{10^4}, P(R\cap E)=\frac{P(5,4)}{10^4}\text{.}\) Thus
    \begin{equation*} P(R\cup E)= P(R)+P(E)-P(R\cap E)=\frac{P(10,4)+5^4-P(5,4)}{10^4}=\frac{5040+625-120}{10^4}=\frac{5545}{10000}=0.5545. \end{equation*}
  2. Note that \(P(R|E)=\frac{P(5,4)/10^4}{5^4/10^4}=\frac{P(5,4)}{5^4}=\frac{120}{625}=0.192\) .
  3. Since \(P(R|E)=0.192\neq0.504=P(R)\) so these are not independent.
Checkpoint 9.3.5.

Suppose you had a bus of 20 people, and 5 are sick, suppose 3 were picked to test at random.

  1. What is the probability that everyone who was tested were all sick?
  2. What is the probability that none are sick?
  3. What is the probability that 2 of the tested individuals were sick?
  4. If at least one person tested sick, what is the probability that were all sick?
Solution

There are \(C(20,3)=1140\) ways to pick 3 people out of 20.

  1. There are \(C(5,3)=10\) ways to pick only sick people, so the probability this happens is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{C(5,3)}{C(20,3)}=\frac{10}{1140}\approx 0.0088. \end{equation*}
  2. There are \(C(15,3)=455\) ways to pick only healthy people, so the probability this happens is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{C(15,3)}{C(20,3)}=\frac{455}{1140}\approx 0.3991. \end{equation*}
  3. There are \(C(5,2)\cdot C(15,1)=10\cdot 15=150\) ways to pick two sick and one healthy person, so the probability this happens is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{C(5,2)\cdot C(15,1)}{C(20,3)}=\frac{10\cdot 15}{1140}\approx 0.1316. \end{equation*}
  4. The probability that at least one person tested sick is \(1-\frac{C(15,3)}{C(20,3)}\text{.}\) Thus assuming this happens, the probability everyone tests sick is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{C(5,3)/C(20,3)}{1-C(15,3)/C(20,3)}\approx \frac{0.0088}{0.6009}\approx 0.0146. \end{equation*}
Checkpoint 9.3.6.

An elevator has 5 passengers and stops at 8 floors. It is equally likely that a person will get off at any one of the 8 floors. Find the probability that at least 2 passengers leave at the same floor.

Solution

There are \(8^5\) ways the passangers could get off the elevator. The probability that everyone gets off at different floors is \(\frac{P(8,5)}{8^5}=\frac{6720}{32768}\approx0.2051\text{.}\) So the probability that this doesn't happen, that is, at least two get off at the same floor, is:

\begin{equation*} 1-\frac{P(8,5)}{8^5}=1-\frac{6720}{32768}\approx0.7949. \end{equation*}
Checkpoint 9.3.7.

There are two jars, jar one has 5 white marbles and 6 black marbles. Jar 2 has 8 white marbles and 7 black marbles. Suppose you pick a jar at random and pick two marbles at random from said jar.

  1. What is the probability both marbles are white?
  2. If both marbles are white, what is the probability Jar 2 was chosen?
Solution

Let \(W\) denote “both marbles are white”, \(J_1\) denote “Jar one was chosen”. Note that \(P(J_1)=0.5\text{.}\)

  1. So by Bayes Theorem:
    \begin{align*} P(W)\amp =P(W|J_1)P(J_1)+P(W|J_1^c)(1-P(J_1))\\ P(W)\amp =\frac{C(5,2)}{C(11,2)}(0.5)+\frac{C(8,2)}{C(15,2)}(0.5)\\ P(W)\amp =\frac{10}{55}(0.5)+\frac{28}{105}(0.5)\\ P(W)\amp =\frac{36}{165}\approx 0.224242 \end{align*}
  2. Note, we assume \(W\text{,}\) so this is \(P(J_2|W)\text{.}\) This is then computed:
    \begin{equation*} P(J_2^c|W)=\frac{P(J_2\cap W)}{P(W)}=\frac{P(W|J_1^c)(1-P(J_1))}{P(W)}=\frac{\frac{28}{105}(0.5)}{\frac{36}{165}}=\frac{11}{18}\approx 0.6111111. \end{equation*}
    After all, white marbles are more likely in Jar 2, so an all white selection is more likely to come from Jar 2.

Section 5.3 Regular Payments

Up until this point, we have discussed one time loans or investments. But the fact is that most loans and investments aren't made or paid off in single lump sums. You may purchase a car or a vehicle, and then slowly pay off that debt over time. Maybe you're lucky enough to recieve an inheritance in an account that you slowly draw money from until it is depleted. Maybe you make regular payments into a retirement or rainy day fund until a certain date or time is reached.

This complicates the process of computing the present and future value of loans and investments. If you make regular payments into an account, then each payment will accrue interest, but for different lengths of time. Similarly, if you have an account earning interest you draw from regularly, then the account is still generating interest, but less and less as you draw the funds out.

In this section, we will address these issues.

Subsection 5.3.1 Future Value

Let's say you have a back account that gives you 2% monthly interest and let's also say that from January to May, you deposit $100 each month. How much will you have in May? Well:

  • The 100 dollars you put in January would accrue interests for 4 months, so you'd have \(100(1.02)^4\) dollars.

  • The 100 dollars you put in February would accrue interests for 3 months, so you'd have \(100(1.02)^3\) dollars.

  • The 100 dollars you put in March would accrue interests for 2 months, so you'd have \(100(1.02)^2\) dollars.

  • The 100 dollars you put in April would accrue interests for 1 months, so you'd have \(100(1.02)^1\) dollars.

  • The 100 dollars you put in May would accrue interests for 0 months, so you'd have \(100(1.02)^0\) dollars.

So you'd have:

\begin{equation*} 100(1.02)^4+100(1.02)^3+100(1.02)^2+100(1.02)^1+100(1.02)^0\approx \$520.40 \end{equation*}

But what if this went on for 10 months? For 10 years? You probably wouldn't want to calculate every single one of these. So is there an easier way to compute something like

\begin{equation*} 100(1.02)^4+100(1.02)^3+100(1.02)^2+100(1.02)^1+100(1.02)^0? \end{equation*}

So we have a sum of things that look like: \(x^n+x^{n-1}+\cdots+x^1+x^0\text{.}\) There is a funny fact about this sum. if we multiply it by \((x-1)\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} \amp\ (x^n+x^{n-1}+\cdots+x^1+x^0)(x-1)\\ \amp = (x^n+x^{n-1}+\cdots+x^1+x^0)x-(x^n+x^{n-1}+\cdots+x^1+x^0)\\ \amp = x^{n+1}+x^n+\cdots+x-x^n-x^{n-1}-\cdots-x^0\\ \amp = x^{n+1}-x^0\\ \amp = x^{n+1}-1 \end{align*}

Thus:

\begin{align*} (x^n+x^{n-1}+\cdots+1)(x-1)\amp = x^{n+1}-1\\ x^n+x^{n-1}+\cdots+1\amp = \frac{x^{n+1}-1}{x-1} \end{align*}

So, we should note that the future value of our investment above is:

\begin{align*} \amp\ 100(1.02)^4+100(1.02)^3+100(1.02)^2+100(1.02)^1+100(1.02)^0\\ \amp = 100((1.02)^4+(1.02)^3+(1.02)^2+(1.02)^1+(1.02)^0)\\ \amp = 100\frac{(1.02)^5-1}{1.02-1}\\ S\amp = 100\frac{(1.02)^5-1}{.02}\approx \$520.40. \end{align*}

Where \(S\) is the future value. In 5 months, you would have \(\$520.40\) in this account!

Now, if we did this for \(n\) months instead of 5, we would obtain:

\begin{equation*} S=100\frac{(1.02)^n-1}{.02} \end{equation*}

If the monthly (or whichever time unit) interest rate was \(i\) instead of 2%, we would obtain:

\begin{equation*} S=100\frac{(1+i)^n-1}{i} \end{equation*}

And if the payments were \(\$R\) instead of $100, we would finally have:

\begin{equation*} S=R\frac{(1+i)^n-1}{i} \end{equation*}

So here we have constructed the formula for the future value of a debt or investment based on a constant payment.

Checkpoint 5.3.1.

Suppose that I deposit $100 into an account at 1% monthly interest compounded monthly. How much will be in the account in 5 years?

Solution

So we have:

\begin{align*} R\amp = 100\\ n\amp = 60\\ i\amp = 0.01 \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{align*} S\amp = 100\frac{(1.01)^{60}-1}{0.01}\approx8166.97 \end{align*}

So in 5 years, this account will be worth \(\$8166.97\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 5.3.2.

Suppose that Aziz borrowed a fixed amount of money per year at a rate of \(12\%\) interest compounded annually. If in 10 years, he owed \(\$20000\text{,}\) how much did he borrow each year?

Solution

So we have:

\begin{align*} S\amp = 20000\\ n\amp = 10\\ i\amp = 0.12 \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{align*} 20000\amp = R\frac{(1.12)^{10}-1}{0.12}\\ 20000\frac{0.12}{1.12^{10}-1}\amp = R\\ R\amp \approx 1139.68 \end{align*}

So each year, Aziz borrowed about \(\$1139.68\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 5.3.3.

Olive wants to deposit \(\$500\) a month into an account, how much monthly interest would this account need to return in order for her to save \(\$8000\) in a year?

Solution

So we have:

\begin{align*} R\amp = 500\\ S\amp = 8000\\ n\amp = 12 \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{align*} 8000\amp = 500\frac{(1+i)^{12}-1}{i}\\ 16\amp = \frac{(1+i)^{12}-1}{i} \end{align*}

Then, rather than engage in a nightmarish algebraic exercise, we will simply plot \(y=\frac{(1+x)^{12}-1}{x}\) and see when it reaches the value \(y=12\text{.}\) We can observe that these curves intersect when \(x\approx 0.0509\text{.}\)

So each year, Olive's account would have to accrue \(5.09\%\) interest.

Checkpoint 5.3.4.

Katya wants to save \(\$2000\) a year into a savings account with \(10\%\) annual interest. How long would she have to save before accumulating \(\$50000\text{?}\)

Solution

So we have:

\begin{align*} R\amp = 2000\\ S\amp = 50000\\ i\amp = 0.10 \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{align*} 50000\amp = 2000\frac{(1.1)^{n}-1}{.1}\\ 2.5\amp = 1.1^n-1\\ 3.5\amp = 1.1^n\\ \ln(3.5)\amp= n\ln(1.1)\\ n\amp = \frac{\ln(3.5)}{\ln(1.1)}\approx 13.144 \end{align*}

We could also plot \(y=1.1^x\) and set this against \(y=3.5\) to see what \(n\) should be: We can observe that these curves intersect when \(x\approx 13.144\text{.}\)

So each year, Katya will have saved \(\$50000\) in just over 13 years.

Subsection 5.3.2 Present Value

Just as a series of regular payments or withdrawals has a future value \(S\text{,}\) so to do they have a present value \(P\text{.}\) So if we have \(n\) payments of \(R\) at regular interest rate \(i\text{,}\) the future value is \(S=R\frac{(1+i)^n-1}{i}\text{.}\) What is it's present value \(P\text{?}\) To see this, recall that in SectionÂ 5.2, we have established the following relationship between present and future value:

\begin{align*} S\amp = P(1+i)^n\\ P\amp = \frac{S}{(1+i)^n}\text{.} \end{align*}

From this point, we may substitute \(S=R\frac{(1+i)^n-1}{i}\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} P\amp = \frac{R\frac{(1+i)^n-1}{i}}{(1+i)^n}\\ \amp = R\frac{((1+i)^n-1)\frac{1}{(1+i)^n}}{i}\\ \amp = R\frac{1-\frac{1}{(1+i)^n}}{i}\\ P\amp = R\frac{1-(1+i)^{-n}}{i}. \end{align*}

Suppose that we invest $100,000 in an account that gives 1% monthly interest and I'd like to pull a regular sum of money out of the account for the next 10 years. What is the maximum that I could pull out?

Well, we have a present value of \(P=100,000\text{,}\) a monthly interest rate of \(i=0.01\) and \(n=120\) months. So:

\begin{align*} 100000\amp = R\frac{1-(1.01)^{-120}}{.01}\\ 1000\amp \approx R(1-0.302995)\\ R\amp \approx \frac{1000}{0.697005}\\ \amp \approx 1434.71 \end{align*}

So with an investment like that, you could pull out \(\$1434.71\) a month for the next 10 years.

Checkpoint 5.3.5.

Zhou wishes to purchase a \(\$200000\) home. After putting down a down payment, he takes out a 20 year mortgage of \(\$160000\) at a monthly rate of \(0.3\%\text{.}\) How much will his monthly payments be?

Solution

So we have:

\begin{align*} P\amp = 160000\\ n\amp = 240\\ i\amp = 0.003 \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{align*} 160000\amp = R\frac{1-(1.003)^{-240}}{.003}\\ 160000\amp \approx R(170.9076)\\ R\amp \approx \frac{160000}{170.9076}\approx 936.18 \end{align*}

So Zhou's monthly mortage payment will be about \(\$936.18\) a month.

Checkpoint 5.3.6.

Tyrone authorizes a loan to a debtor who will make payments of \(\$1000\) a year for 10 years at \(6\%\) interest. How much was the loan for?

Solution

So we have:

\begin{align*} R\amp = 1000\\ n\amp = 10\\ i\amp = 0.06 \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{align*} P\amp = 1000\frac{1-(1.06)^{-10}}{.06}\\ \amp \approx 7360.09. \end{align*}

So the loan would have been for \(\$7360.09\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 5.3.7.

Karen racked up \(\$4000\) in credit card debit with a \(2\%\) monthly interest rate. She can only make \(\$100\) monthly payments on it. How long will it take for her to pay this debt off?

Solution

So we have:

\begin{align*} R\amp = 100\\ P\amp = 4000\\ i\amp = 0.02 \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{align*} 4000\amp = 100\frac{1-(1.02)^{-n}}{.02}\\ 40 \amp = \frac{1-(1.02)^{-n}}{.02}\\ 0.8 \amp = 1-(1.02)^{-n}\\ (1.02)^{-n} \amp = 0.2\\ -n\ln((1.02)) \amp = \ln(0.2)\\ n\amp = \frac{\ln(0.2)}{-\ln(1.02)}\approx 81.274 \end{align*}

Or, by plotting \(y=\frac{1-(1.02)^{-x}}{.02}\) and \(y=40\text{,}\) we can see what the value of \(n\) should be: \(n\approx 81.274\text{.}\)

So she would have paid the loan off in 82 months, 7 years and 10 months.

Checkpoint 5.3.8.

Jasmine lends \(\$2500\) to a friend who will make \(\$200\) weekly payments on it for 15 weeks. What in effect is the weekly interest rate of her loan?

Solution

So we have:

\begin{align*} R\amp = 200\\ P\amp = 2500\\ n\amp = 15 \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{align*} 2500\amp = 200\frac{1-(1+i)^{-15}}{i}\\ 12.5\amp = \frac{1-(1+i)^{-15}}{i} \end{align*}

So by plotting \(y=\frac{1-(1+x)^{-15}}{x}\) and \(y=12.5\text{,}\) we can see what the value of \(i\) should be: \(i\approx 0.0237\text{.}\)

So the effective weekly interest she charged was \(2.37\%\text{.}\)

Section 7.6 Derivative of Exponentials

When we graph an exponential function, say an increasing one \(f(x)=a^x, a>1\text{,}\) we imagine a function that curves up towards infinity as \(x\) increases and converges to 0 as \(x\) decreases. What then should it's derivatives look like? Well it seems like the slopes are initially shallow, and then as we go forward the slopes start to increase, and they get progressively and progressively larger. In other words the derivative is also an exponential function!

Subsection 7.6.1 Computing the derivative of Exponentials

Which one? Let's first start with a special case, what is the derivative of \(f(x)=e^x\text{?}\) \(e\) is a special number, it has the peculiar property that:

\begin{equation*} \limit{h}{0}\frac{e^h-1}{h}=1\text{.} \end{equation*}

The proof of this is a fairly sophisticated fact and is not trivial, but we may still visualize the limit itself: Using this fact, we can deduce:

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}[e^x]\amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{e^{x+h}-e^x}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{e^xe^h-e^x}{h}\\ \amp = e^x\limit{h}{0}\frac{e^h-1}{h}\\ \amp = e^x\cdot1=e^x\text{.} \end{align*}

So this explains one of the reaons why \(e\) is such a significant number in mathematics, it is the unique number so that the exponential \(e^x\) is it's own derivative.

So, to compute the actual derivative of \(a^x\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}[a^x]\amp = \frac{d}{dx}[(e^{\ln(a)})^x],\ \text{ since exponentiating by \(e\) and \(\ln\) are inverses }\\ \amp = \frac{d}{dx}[e^{\ln(a)x}]\\ \amp = e^{\ln(a)x}\frac{d}{dx}[\ln(a)x],\ \text{ by chain rule }\\ \amp = e^{\ln(a)x}\ln(a)\cdot1\\ \amp = a^x\ln(a)\text{.} \end{align*}
Example 7.6.1. Derivatives involving exponentials.

Let's try the derivative of \(f(x)=e^{x}x^2\) and \(h(x)=2^{\frac{x}{x+1}}\)

Solution

We first have:

\begin{align*} f'(x)\amp = \frac{d}{dx}[e^xx^2]\\ \amp =_{PR} \frac{d}{dx}[e^x]x^2+e^x\frac{d}{dx}[x^2]\\ \amp = e^xx^2+e^x2x\text{.} \end{align*}

Next, we compute:

\begin{align*} h'(x)\amp = \frac{d}{dx}[2^{\frac{x}{x+1}}]\\ \amp =_{CR} 2^{\frac{x}{x+1}}\ln(2)\frac{d}{dx}[\frac{x}{x+1}]\\ \amp =_{QR} 2^{\frac{x}{x+1}}\ln(2)\frac{(x+1)\frac{d}{dx}[x]-x\frac{d}{dx}[x+1]}{(x+1)^2}\\ \amp = \frac{2^{\frac{x}{x+1}}\ln(2)}{(x+1)^2} \end{align*}
Checkpoint 7.6.2.

The daily revenue of a local convient store, \(t\) days after opening is \(R(t)=10-8e^{-0.02t}\) in thousands of dollars. How is the daily revenue increasing or decreasing after 30 days?

Solution

We note that:

\begin{align*} R'(t)\amp = \frac{d}{dt}[10-8e^{-0.02t}]\\ \amp = 0-8e^{-0.02t}\frac{d}{dt}[-0.02t]\\ \amp = -8e^{-0.02t}(-0.02)\\ \amp = 0.16e^{-0.02t}\text{.} \end{align*}

Thus \(R'(30)=0.16e^{-0.02(30)}\approx 0.088\) so increasing by about \(\$88 \) a day.

Checkpoint 7.6.3.

The quantity of a drug injected in a patient is

\begin{equation*} D(t)=te^{-0.25t} \end{equation*}

where \(D\) is measured in mL and \(t\) is in minutes after injection.

  1. How is the drug quantity changing in 2 minutes?
  2. When does the drug quantity start to decrease?
Solution

We note that:

\begin{align*} D'(t)\amp = \frac{d}{dt}[te^{-0.25t}]\\ \amp = t\frac{d}{dt}[e^{-0.25t}]+\frac{d}{dt}[t]e^{-0.25t}\\ \amp = te^{-0.25t}\frac{d}{dt}[-0.25t]+1\cdot e^{-0.25t}\\ \amp = te^{-0.25t}\cdot(-0.25t)+e^{-0.25t}\\ \amp = (1-0.25t)e^{-0.25t}\text{.} \end{align*}

So with that:

  1. Notce \(D'(2)=(1-0.25(2))e^{-0.25(2)}\approx 0.303 \) or increasing by 0.303 mL/minute.
  2. We note that the quantity decreases when \(D'(t)=(1-0.25t)e^{-0.25t}\lt 0\) so:
    \begin{align*} (1-0.25t)e^{-0.25t}\amp \lt 0\\ (1-0.25t)\amp \lt 0\\ 1\amp \lt 0.25t\\ t\amp > 4\text{.} \end{align*}

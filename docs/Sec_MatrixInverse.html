Section 2.4 Matrix Inverses

In Definition 2.3.5, we saw the introduction of an identity matrix. This matrix plays the role of multiplying by 1. So, given another matrix \(A\text{,}\) is there a matrix \(B\) so that \(AB=I_n\text{?}\)

Subsection 2.4.1 Direct Inversion

We begin with a motivating example: Consider matrices

\begin{equation*} A=\begin{pmatrix} 4 \amp 1 \\ 3 \amp 1\end{pmatrix}, B=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp -1 \\ -3 \amp 4\end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}

When we take their products, we notice:

Thus \(AB=I_2=BA\text{.}\) Thus \(A\) and \(B\) are inverses of each other.

Definition 2.4.1.

Given an \(n\times n\) matrix \(A\text{,}\) it's inverse is an \(n\times n\) matrix denoted \(A^{-1}\) such that

\begin{equation*} AA^{-1}=I_n=A^{-1}A. \end{equation*}

So lets say we wanted to find the inverse of

\begin{equation*} M= \begin{pmatrix} 3 \amp 1 \\ 5 \amp 2\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

we want a matrix \(M^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix} a \amp b \\ c \amp d\end{pmatrix}\) such that

\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 4 \amp 1 \\ 3 \amp 1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} a \amp b \\ c \amp d\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1\end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}

Thus \(a,b,c,d\) must satisfy:

\begin{align*} 3a+c\amp=1\\ 3b+d\amp=0\\ 5a+2c\amp=0\\ 5b+2d\amp=1 \end{align*}

So solving this system:

Gives us \(a=2, b=-1, c=-5, d=3\text{.}\) Thus

\begin{equation*} M^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix} 2 \amp -1 \\ -5 \amp 3\end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}

Subsection 2.4.2 Non-Invertible Matrices

Suppose we had

\begin{equation*} M=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ -1 \amp -1\end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}

If we tried to find it's inverse as we have above, we would have:

\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ -1 \amp -1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} a \amp b \\ c \amp d\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1\end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}

and the system

\begin{align*} a-c\amp=1\\ b-d\amp=0\\ a-c\amp=0\\ b-d\amp=1 \end{align*}

But wait! How can \(a-c=1\) and \(a-c=0\text{?}\) This is a non-solvable system. We can see it here as well:

We see that we have a row \(0a+0b+0c+0d=1\text{,}\) which has no solution.

Remark 2.4.2.
Not every matrix is invertible, such matrices are called singular matrices.

Checkpoint 2.4.3. Blending Juice.

The Acme Juice Company makes three juice blends: PineOrange, using 2 quarts of pineapple juice and 2 quarts of orange juice per gallon; PineKiwi, using 3 quarts of pineapple juice and 1 quart of kiwi juice per gallon; and OrangeKiwi, using 3 quarts of orange juice and 1 quart of kiwi juice per gallon. The amount of each kind of juice the company has on hand varies from day to day. How many gallons of each blend can it make on a day with the following stocks?

  1. 400 quarts of pineapple juice, 400 quarts of orange juice and 100 quarts of kiwi juice.
  2. 400 quarts of pineapple juice, 325 quarts of orange juice and 175 quarts of kiwi juice.
  3. 140 quarts of pineapple juice, 440 quarts of orange juice and 140 quarts of kiwi juice.
Solution 1

Consider the matrix \(A\) which represents a transformation from Products to Ingredients:

\begin{equation*} A=\begin{pmatrix} 2\amp 3 \amp 0 \\ 2\amp 0 \amp 3 \\ 0\amp 1 \amp 1\end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}

We can compute the inverse matrix from Ingredients to Products:

\begin{equation*} A^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{4}\amp \frac{1}{4} \amp -\frac{3}{4} \\ \frac{1}{6}\amp -\frac{1}{6} \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ -\frac{1}{6}\amp \frac{1}{6} \amp \frac{1}{2}\end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}

So if we had 400 quarts of pineapple juice, 400 quarts of orange juice and 100 quarts of kiwi juice, the amount of product we could make would be:

\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{4}\amp \frac{1}{4} \amp -\frac{3}{4} \\ \frac{1}{6}\amp -\frac{1}{6} \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ -\frac{1}{6}\amp \frac{1}{6} \amp \frac{1}{2}\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 400\\400\\100 \end{pmatrix}= \begin{pmatrix}125 \\ 50\\50\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

That is, 125 gallons of PineOrange, 50 gallons of PineKiwi and 50 gallons of OrangeKiwi.

Solution 2

If we had 400 quarts of pineapple juice, 325 quarts of orange juice and 175 quarts of kiwi juice, the amount of product we could make would be:

\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{4}\amp \frac{1}{4} \amp -\frac{3}{4} \\ \frac{1}{6}\amp -\frac{1}{6} \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ -\frac{1}{6}\amp \frac{1}{6} \amp \frac{1}{2}\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 400\\325\\175 \end{pmatrix}= \begin{pmatrix}50 \\ 100\\75\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

That is, 50 gallons of PineOrange, 100 gallons of PineKiwi and 75 gallons of OrangeKiwi.

Solution 3

If we had 140 quarts of pineapple juice, 440 quarts of orange juice and 140 quarts of kiwi juice, the amount of product we could make would be:

\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{4}\amp \frac{1}{4} \amp -\frac{3}{4} \\ \frac{1}{6}\amp -\frac{1}{6} \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ -\frac{1}{6}\amp \frac{1}{6} \amp \frac{1}{2}\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 140\\440\\140 \end{pmatrix}= \begin{pmatrix}40 \\ 20\\120\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

That is, 40 gallons of PineOrange, 20 gallons of PineKiwi and 120 gallons of OrangeKiwi.

Section 7.3 The Derivative Function

In Section 7.2, we discuss average rates of change over intervals. But typically, when rates of change are discussed, it's not described in terms of periods of time but at the very moment. What is the speed right now? Not what is the average speed? How fast is the temperature rising? How quickly is the Dow falling? These are all described as instantaneous rates of change. In other words, rates of change as the interval over which they are defined tends to zero.

Subsection 7.3.1 From Average to Instant

Recall that in Example 7.2.2, we found the average rate of change in the height, \(f(x)=-5x^2+20x\text{,}\) of a rocket \(x\) seconds after launch , over the intervals \([0,2], [0,1], [0,0.5]\text{.}\) But what prompted this discussion was what the speed was at the moment of launch. We can't actually evaluate the average rate of change over \([0,0]\text{,}\) but what we can do is find the average rate of change over \([0, h]\) and see what happens as \(h\to 0\text{.}\) As you can observe, it appears as if this slope approaches 20 m/s.

More formally, this value would be \(\limit{h}{0} \frac{f(0+h)-f(0)}{h}\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} \limit{h}{0}\frac{f(0+h)-f(0)}{h}\amp=\limit{h}{0}\frac{-5h^2+20h}{h}\\ \amp=\limit{h}{0}\frac{(-5h+20)h}{h}\\ \amp=\limit{h}{0}-5h+20=20. \end{align*}

So 20 m/s is in fact the instantaneous rate of change of the rocket at time \(x=0\text{.}\)

Definition 7.3.1.

The instantaneous rate of change of \(f(x)\) at \(a\) denoted \(f'(a)\) or the derivative of \(f(x)\) at \(a\) is:

\begin{equation*} f'(a):=\limit{h}{0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}. \end{equation*}
Checkpoint 7.3.2.

Let \(\alpha(x)=\frac{2}{x+1}\text{.}\) Find \(\alpha'(2)\text{.}\)

Solution

By Definition 7.3.1 we have:

\begin{align*} \alpha'(2)\amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{\alpha(2+h)-\alpha(2)}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{\frac{2}{2+h+1}-\frac{2}{2+1}}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{\frac{2}{h+3}-\frac{2}{3}}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{\frac{2\cdot 3}{(h+3)3}-\frac{2(h+3)}{3(h+3)}}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{\frac{6-2(h+3)}{(h+3)3}}{h} \\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{\frac{6-2(h+3)}{(h+3)3}}{h} \\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{\frac{-2h}{(h+3)3}}{h} \\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{-2}{(h+3)3} \\ \amp = \frac{-2}{(0+3)3}=-\frac{2}{9} \text{.} \end{align*}

Checkpoint 7.3.3.

The area of an oil spill \(t\) hours after a pipe burst is \(A(t)=1000\pi\sqrt{t}\) square feet. How fast is the spill spreading in 10 hours?

Solution

By Definition 7.3.1 we have:

\begin{align*} A'(10)\amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{A(2+h)-A(2)}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{1000\pi\sqrt{10+h}-1000\pi\sqrt{10}}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 1000\pi\frac{\sqrt{10+h}-\sqrt{10}}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 1000\pi\frac{(\sqrt{10+h}-\sqrt{10})(\sqrt{10+h}+\sqrt{10})}{h(\sqrt{10+h}+\sqrt{10})}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 1000\pi\frac{10+h-10}{h(\sqrt{10+h}+\sqrt{10})}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 1000\pi\frac{h}{h(\sqrt{10+h}+\sqrt{10})}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 1000\pi\frac{1}{(\sqrt{10+h}+\sqrt{10})}\\ \amp = 1000\pi\frac{1}{(\sqrt{10+0}+\sqrt{10})}=\frac{1000\pi}{2\sqrt{10}}\approx 496.729\text{.} \end{align*}

Subsection 7.3.2 Dervative as a function in it's own right

We note that in our rocket situation, there is nothing particularly special about time \(x=0\text{.}\) In principle, the derivative described in Definition 7.3.1 could be applied to any value of \(x\text{.}\) Also, the rocket has a velocity at any time, and we should be able to compute it. If we apply Definition 7.3.1 to a general \(f(x)\text{,}\) we obtain:

\begin{equation*} f'(x)=\limit{h}{0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\text{.} \end{equation*}

So how does this play out given our rocket? Well:

\begin{align*} f'(x)\amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{-5(x+h)^2+20(x+h)-(-5x^2+20x)}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{-5x^2-10xh-5h^2+20x+20h+5x^2-20x}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{-10xh-5h^2+20h}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{(-10x-5h+20)h}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} -10x-5h+20\\ \amp = -10x+20\text{.} \end{align*}

So this means that in \(0\) seconds, the change in the height was \(f'(0)=-10(0)+20=20\) meters/second as we suspected! But now we can compute all the other instantaneous rates of change as well.

  • So at \(x=1\) second, it'll be \(f'(1)=-10(1)+20=10\) meters per second.
  • In 2 seconds we are at the top with \(f'(2)=0\) meters per second.
  • At 3 seconds, \(f'(3)=-10\) meters/second to reflect the negative change in height at this point.
  • Finally in 4 seconds, the rocket hits the ground at \(f'(4)=-20\) meters per second.

One can explore this below in the Desmos graph. Notice how \(y=f'(x), f=-10x+20\) produce the same line:

Checkpoint 7.3.4.

Let \(\alpha(x)=\frac{2}{x+1}\text{.}\) Find \(\alpha'(x)\text{.}\)

Solution

We have:

\begin{align*} \alpha'(x)\amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{\alpha(x+h)-\alpha(x)}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{\frac{2}{x+h+1}-\frac{2}{x+1}}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{\frac{2(x+1)}{(x+h+1)(x)}-\frac{2(x+h+1)}{(x+h+1)(x+1)}}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{\frac{2(x+1)}{(x+h+1)(x)}-\frac{2(x+h+1)}{(x+h+1)(x+1)}}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{\frac{2(x+1)-2(x+h+1)}{(x+h+1)(x+1)}}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{\frac{2x+2-2x-2h-2}{(x+h+1)(x+1)}}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{\frac{-2h}{(x+h+1)(x+1)}}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{-2}{(x+h+1)(x+1)}\\ \amp = \frac{-2}{(x+0+1)(x+1)}=-\frac{2}{(x+1)^2}\text{.} \end{align*}

We can see this below:

Checkpoint 7.3.5.

The area of an oil spill \(t\) hours after a pipe burst is \(A(t)=1000\pi\sqrt{t}\) square feet. Find \(A'(t)\text{.}\)

Solution

We have:

\begin{align*} A'(t)\amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{A(t+h)-A(t)}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{1000\pi\sqrt{t+h}-1000\pi\sqrt{t}}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 1000\pi\frac{\sqrt{t+h}-\sqrt{t}}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 1000\pi\frac{(\sqrt{t+h}-\sqrt{t})(\sqrt{t+h}+\sqrt{t})}{h(\sqrt{t+h}+\sqrt{t})}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 1000\pi\frac{t+h-t}{h(\sqrt{t+h}+\sqrt{t})}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 1000\pi\frac{h}{h(\sqrt{t+h}+\sqrt{t})}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 1000\pi\frac{1}{(\sqrt{t+h}+\sqrt{t})}\\ \amp = 1000\pi\frac{1}{(\sqrt{t+0}+\sqrt{t})}=\frac{1000\pi}{2\sqrt{t}}=\frac{500\pi}{\sqrt{t}}\text{.} \end{align*}

Subsection 7.3.3 Graphical Interpretation of the Derivative

Remark 7.3.6.

Since the derivative corresponds to an instantaneous rate of change, given a function \(f(x)\) we have:

  • \(f'(a)>0\) when \(f(x)\) is increasing at \(x=a\text{.}\)
  • \(f'(a)\lt 0\) when \(f(x)\) is decreasing at \(x=a\text{.}\)
  • \(f'(a)=0\) when \(f(x)\) is neither increasing nor decreasing at \(x=a\text{.}\)

So consider the curve depicted below: Observing this graph, we have that the function is decreasing (\(f'(x)\lt 0\)) from 1 to 4, or the interval \((1,4)\text{.}\) The derivatve is positive when \(f(x)\) is increasing, that is \((-\infty,1)\cup(4,\infty)\text{.}\) At \(x=1, 4\text{,}\) we have that \(f'(1)=0, f'(4)=0\text{.}\)

Section 9.4 Probability Distrbutions

Up to this point, we have mostly focused on the probability of specific events. In this section, we take a step back and look at the experiment itself. Certainly, if I have a jar with 5 red and 7 blue balls and draw 3 without replacement, there is a chance that I get 2 red balls. But now we want to ask, what number of red balls are more or less likely? On average, how many red balls would I get? How varied are the results?

Subsection 9.4.1 Random Variables

Definition 9.4.1.

A random variable is a function that assigns a real number to each element of the sample space.

Example 9.4.2.

Consider the experiment rolling two dice. We can see that the sample space for this experiment is:

\begin{equation*} (1,1),(1,2),(1,3),(1,4),(1,5),(1,6) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} (2,1),(2,2),(2,3),(2,4),(2,5),(2,6) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} (3,1),(3,2),(3,3),(3,4),(3,5),(3,6) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} (4,1),(4,2),(4,3),(4,4),(4,5),(4,6) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} (5,1),(5,2),(5,3),(5,4),(5,5),(5,6) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} (6,1),(6,2),(6,3),(6,4),(6,5),(6,6) \end{equation*}

So if we consider the random variable \(X\) to be “sum of die outcomes”, we would have the assignment:

\begin{equation*} 2,3,4,5,6,7 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 3,4,5,6,7,8 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 4,5,6,7,8,9 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 5,6,7,8,9,10 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 6,7,8,9,10,11 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 7,8,9,10,11,12 \end{equation*}

On the other hand, if the random variable \(Y\) to be “second die outcome”, we would have the assignment:

\begin{equation*} 1,2,3,4,5,6 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 1,2,3,4,5,6 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 1,2,3,4,5,6 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 1,2,3,4,5,6 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 1,2,3,4,5,6 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 1,2,3,4,5,6 \end{equation*}

There are many more possibilities of course, but one last one, if \(Z\) denoted “larger of die rolls” we would have:

\begin{equation*} 1,2,3,4,5,6 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 2,2,3,4,5,6 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 3,3,3,4,5,6 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 4,4,4,4,5,6 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 5,5,5,5,5,6 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 6,6,6,6,6,6 \end{equation*}

A probability distribution is, roughly speaking, a complete description of a random variable and the likelihood of each output. In the case of random variables with a finite number of possible outputs a probability distribution table is a convenient way of presenting this information.

So let \(X\) denote “the outcome of a die roll”. We have that \(X\) could take on the values \(1,\ldots, 6\) and for each of these values, say \(1\text{,}\) the probability \(X=1\) is \(P(X=1)=\frac{1}{6}\text{.}\) This gives us:

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|cccccc|} \hline x \amp 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \\ \hline P(X=x) \amp \frac{1}{6} \amp \frac{1}{6} \amp \frac{1}{6} \amp \frac{1}{6} \amp \frac{1}{6} \amp \frac{1}{6}\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

What does this mean concretely? It means that if one were to roll a dice \(n\) times, we would expect about a sixth of the outcomes to correspond to each of the numbers 1 through 6. See the interaction below, hit the refresh on the top left.

Remark 9.4.3.

To check if something is a valid probability distribution, for any possible outcome \(x\) of \(X\) we must have:

  • \(0\leq P(X=x)\leq1\text{.}\) This ensures all outcomes are valid probabilities.
  • \(\sum P(X=x)=1\text{.}\) The sum of the probabilities of all outcomes should be 100% of the outcomes
Example 9.4.4.

Suppose you buy a ticket for a game of chance. You have a \(1/10\) chance of winning the grand prize \(\$20\) and a \(3/10\) chance of winning the runner up prize \(\$5\text{.}\) Find a probability distribution for \(X\) your winnings. What is the probability you win something?

Solution

The chance you win nothing would be \(1-\frac{1}{10}-\frac{3}{10}=\frac{6}{10}\text{.}\) So the probability distribution would be:

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|ccc|} \hline x \amp 0 \amp 5 \amp 20 \\ \hline P(X=x) \amp \frac{6}{10} \amp \frac{3}{10} \amp \frac{1}{10} \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

There is a \(\frac{3}{10}+\frac{1}{10}\) chance you win something.

Example 9.4.5.

Snow white has a basket of 10 apples, 3 are poisened. She is going to pick 4 apples at random to eat for some reason. let \(X\) denote the number of poisened apples she eats. Find a probability distribution for \(X\text{.}\) What's the probability she eats at least 2 poisened apples?

Solution

There are 3 poisened apples and 7 non-poisened apples. Each selection ensures she is picking some number of poisened and the rest non poisened. She can't pick more than 3 poisened apples since that's all there is. So the probability distribution would be:

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|cccc|} \hline x \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ \hline P(X=x) \amp \frac{C(3,0)C(7,4)}{C(10,4)} \amp \frac{C(3,1)C(7,3)}{C(10,4)} \amp \frac{C(3,2)C(7,2)}{C(10,4)} \amp \frac{C(3,3)C(7,1)}{C(10,4)}\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

Or:

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|cccc|} \hline x \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ \hline P(X=x) \amp \frac{35}{210}\approx 0.1667\amp \frac{105}{210}=0.5 \amp \frac{63}{210}=0.3 \amp \frac{7}{210}\approx 0.0333\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

There is a \(\frac{63}{210}+\frac{7}{210}=\frac{1}{3}\) chance she eats at least 2 poisened apples.

Subsection 9.4.2 Expectation, Variance and Standard Deviation

Revsiting the dice example above, whats the mean of our 100 die rolls? As I repeat this, does it change much?

We can see that repeatng the experiment more or less gives us an average outcome of about mid 3-something, we should also see that the “spread” of the outcomes (the standard deviation and variance) also doesn't change much either. If we look at Example 9.4.4, we can see the same happens here.

Definition 9.4.6.

Given a finite random variable \(X\text{,}\) it's expected value is the predicted average outcome of experiments, and is computed:

\begin{equation*} E(X)=\sum P(X=x)\cdot x. \end{equation*}

We think of this as the outcomes of \(X\text{,}\) weighted by their likelihood, so the more likely outcomes contribute more than the less likely ones.

Note that the “Expected Value” may not be a value we actually expect, that is, may not be one of the outcomes, just an average outcome.

So in our die rolling case, the expected average outcome would be computed:

\begin{equation*} E(X)=\frac{1}{6}\cdot 1+\frac{1}{6}\cdot 2+\frac{1}{6}\cdot 3+\frac{1}{6}\cdot 4+\frac{1}{6}\cdot 5+\frac{1}{6}\cdot 6=\frac{21}{6}=3.5. \end{equation*}
Definition 9.4.7.

Given a finite random variable \(X\text{,}\) it's variance is a measure of the “spread” or variation of the outcomes, and is computed:

\begin{equation*} Var(X)=E(X^2)-E(X). \end{equation*}

The standard deviation is the square root of the variance, denoted \(\sigma_X\text{.}\)

The difference between standard deviation and variance, and the exact things they measure, are somehwat beyond the scope of this text. The best way to think of the variance computation, is as a way of measuring expected distance of outcomes from the mean.

So in our die rolling case, we already have \(E(X)=3.5\text{,}\) we then compute:

\begin{equation*} E(X^2)=\frac{1}{6}\cdot 1^2+\frac{1}{6}\cdot 2^2+\frac{1}{6}\cdot 3^2+\frac{1}{6}\cdot 4^2+\frac{1}{6}\cdot 5^2+\frac{1}{6}\cdot 6^2=\frac{91}{6}. \end{equation*}

So we have the variance of \(X\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} Var(X)=\frac{91}{6}-(3.5)^2=\frac{35}{12}\approx 2.91667. \end{equation*}

Finally the standard deviation of \(X\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} \sigma_X=\sqrt{\frac{35}{12}}\approx 1.7078. \end{equation*}

Example 9.4.8.

Recall Example 9.4.4. How much should the ticket cost so the game breaks even on average?

Solution

We first need to know the expected winnings, if we recall the probability dstrbution:

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|ccc|} \hline x \amp 0 \amp 5 \amp 20 \\ \hline P(X=x) \amp \frac{6}{10} \amp \frac{3}{10} \amp \frac{1}{10} \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

We have \(E(X)=0\cdot(0.6)+5\cdot(0.3)+20\cdot(0.1)=3.5\text{.}\) So the game returns \(\$3.50\) on average, so charging \(\$3.50\) would make it break even.

Example 9.4.9.

Recall Example 9.4.5. What is the expected value and variance of poisened apples.

Solution

Recall the probability distribution of poisened apples.

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|cccc|} \hline x \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ \hline P(X=x) \amp \frac{35}{210}\approx 0.1667\amp \frac{105}{210}=0.5 \amp \frac{63}{210}=0.3 \amp \frac{7}{210}\approx 0.0333\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

So the expected value of poisened apples would be

\begin{equation*} E(X)=0\cdot\frac{35}{210}+1\cdot\frac{=105}{210}+2\cdot\frac{63}{210}+3\cdot\frac{7}{210}=1.2. \end{equation*}

Then

\begin{equation*} E(X^2)=0^2\cdot\frac{35}{210}+1^2\cdot\frac{=105}{210}+2^2\cdot\frac{63}{210}+3^3\cdot\frac{7}{210}=2. \end{equation*}

So

\begin{equation*} Var(X)=E(X^2)-E(X)^2=2-1.2^2=0.56. \end{equation*}

Checkpoint 9.4.10.

Out of every 20 products on an assembly line, 5 are checked for defects. Suppose in this group of 20, 3 are defective. Let \(X\) denote the number of defective products.

  1. Find a probability distribution for \(X\text{.}\)
  2. Find the expected number of defective products found.
  3. Find \(\sigma_X\text{.}\)
Solution

So there are 3 defective and 17 non-defective products.

  1. We note that each selection comes from either defective or non-defective products. So:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|cccc|} \hline x \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ \hline P(X=x) \amp \frac{C(3,0)C(17,5)}{C(20,5)}\amp \frac{C(3,1)C(17,4)}{C(20,5)} \amp \frac{C(3,2)C(17,3)}{C(20,5)} \amp \frac{C(3,3)C(17,2)}{C(20,5)}\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    This may be rewritten
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|cccc|} \hline x \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ \hline P(X=x) \amp \frac{6188}{15504}\amp \frac{7140}{15504} \amp \frac{2040}{15504} \amp \frac{136}{15504}\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
  2. This would be computed:
    \begin{equation*} E(X)=0\cdot\frac{6188}{15504}+1\cdot\frac{7140}{15504}+2\cdot\frac{2040}{15504}+3\cdot\frac{136}{15504}=0.75. \end{equation*}
  3. We first note:
    \begin{equation*} E(X^2)=0^2\cdot\frac{6188}{15504}+1^2\cdot\frac{7140}{15504}+2^2\cdot\frac{2040}{15504}+3^2\cdot\frac{136}{15504}=\frac{81}{76}\approx 1.0658. \end{equation*}
    This leads:
    \begin{equation*} Var(X)=E(X^2)-E(X)^2=\frac{81}{76}-0.75^2=\frac{153}{304}\approx0.5033. \end{equation*}
    Finally:
    \begin{equation*} \sigma_X=\sqrt{Var(X)}=\sqrt{\frac{153}{304}}\approx 0.7094. \end{equation*}

Checkpoint 9.4.11.

An insurance policy on an electrical device pays a benefit of \(\$3000\) if the device fails during the first year. The amount of the benefit decreases by \(\$1000\) each successive year until it reaches \(\$0\)m>. If the device has not failed by the beginning of any given year, the probability of failure during that year is 0.3. What is the expected benefit under this policy?

Solution

We note that the device has a \(0.3\) chance to fail in the first year, \((0.7)(0.3)\) chance to fail the second since it had to survive the first, \((0.7)^2(0.3)\) to fail the third year, after which the policy ends. So if \(X\) is the payout, then it has distribution

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|cccc|} \hline x \amp 3000 \amp 2000 \amp 1000 \amp 0 \\ \hline P(X=x) \amp (0.3) \amp (0.7)(0.3) \amp (0.7)^2(0.3) \amp 1-(0.3)-(0.7)(0.3)-(0.7)^2(0.3)\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

Which can be rewritten

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|cccc|} \hline x \amp 3000 \amp 2000 \amp 1000 \amp 0 \\ \hline P(X=x) \amp 0.3 \amp 0.21 \amp 0.147 \amp 0.343\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

Which has expected value

\begin{equation*} E(x)=3000\cdot0.3+2000\cdot0.21+1000\cdot0.147+0\cdot0.343=1467. \end{equation*}

So an expected payout of \(\$1467\text{:}\)

Section 2.3 Products and Sums of Matrices

In the previous sections, we saw how the entries of matrix may be used to record relationships between two collections of variables. For example in Example 2.2.3, the entries in the \(3\times 3\) portion of the matrix represents the proportion of vitamins contained by a collection of supplements, with each entry representing a vitamin-supplement pair. It is natural then, to wonder if we could combine relationships between variables or compose them. The operations behind these ideas are the sums and products of matrices.

Subsection 2.3.1 Products of Matrices

We begin with a motivating example:

Example 2.3.1. Vitamins and Supplements.

Recall that in Example 2.2.3 Margo has three supplements: the first contains 20% vitamin A, 20% vitamin D and 20% vitamin E; the second contains 10% vitamin A, 30% vitamin D and 40% vitamin E; the third contains 50% vitamin A, 10% vitamin D and 20% vitamin E.

On Monday she eats 100 mg of supplement 1, 100 mg of supplement 2 and 150 mg of supplement 3. On Wednesday she eats 150 mg of supplement 1, 50 mg of supplement 2 and 100 mg of supplement 3. Putting aside that this is an ill advised way to take supplements, how much of each vitamin did she get on each day?

We first let

\begin{equation*} A= \left( \begin{array}{rrr} 0.2 \amp 0.1 \amp 0.5\\ 0.2 \amp 0.3 \amp 0.1\\ 0.2 \amp 0.4 \amp 0.2 \end{array} \right) \end{equation*}

represent the relation between vitamins and supplements, where each row corresponds to vitamins, and each column corresponds to supplements. So the entry in the second row, second column corresponds to the proportion of supplement 2 which is vitamin D, 30%.

A useful way to conceptualize this matrix is as an arrow or transformation, whose inputs are mg of various supplements, and whose outputs are mg of various vitamins.

Next, we record the relationship between days of the week and supplements:

\begin{equation*} B= \left( \begin{array}{rrr} 100 \amp 150\\ 100 \amp 50\\ 150 \amp 100 \end{array} \right) \end{equation*}

Where now the rows are quantities of supplements and columns are days of the week. So the 100 in the third row second column represents the 100mg taken on Wednesday of Supplement 3. This to may be conceptualized as an arrow or transformation whose inputs are days of the week, and whose outputs are quantities of supplements.

Our goal now is to establish a relationship between days of the week and quantity of vitamins.

So, how much Vitamin A does Margo get on Monday? She took 100 mg of Supplement 1, of which 20% is Vitamin A, 100 mg of Supplement 2, of which 10% is Vitamin A, and 150 mg of Supplement 1, of which 50% is Vitamin A:

\begin{equation*} \left( \begin{array}{rrr} \color{red}{0.2} \amp 0.1 \amp 0.5\\ 0.2 \amp 0.3 \amp 0.1\\ 0.2 \amp 0.4 \amp 0.2 \end{array} \right)\left( \begin{array}{rrr} \color{blue}{100} \amp 150\\ \color{blue}{100} \amp 50\\ \color{blue}{150} \amp 100 \end{array} \right)=\left( \begin{array}{cr} \color{red}{0.2}\cdot\color{blue}{100}+\color{red}{0.1}\cdot\color{blue}{100}+\color{red}{0.5}\cdot\color{blue}{150} \amp ? \\ ? \amp ? \\ ? \amp ? \end{array} \right)=\left( \begin{array}{cr} 105 \amp ? \\ ? \amp ? \\ ? \amp ? \end{array} \right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

So on Monday, Margo got 105 mg of vitamin A. What about the amount of vitamin D she received on Wednesday? She took 150 mg of Supplement 1, of which 20% is Vitamin D, 50 mg of Supplement 2, of which 30% is Vitamin D, and 100 mg of Supplement 1, of which 10% is Vitamin A:

\begin{align*} \left( \begin{array}{rrr} 0.2 \amp 0.1 \amp 0.5\\ \color{red}{0.2} \amp \color{red}{0.3} \amp \color{red}{0.1}\\ 0.2 \amp 0.4 \amp 0.2 \end{array} \right)\left( \begin{array}{rrr} 100 \amp \color{blue}{150}\\ 100 \amp \color{blue}{50}\\ 150 \amp \color{blue}{100} \end{array} \right)\amp =\amp \left( \begin{array}{cr} 105 \amp ? \\ ? \amp \color{red}{0.2}\cdot\color{blue}{150}+\color{red}{0.3}\cdot\color{blue}{50}+\color{red}{0.1}\cdot\color{blue}{100} \\ ? \amp ? \end{array} \right)\\ \amp =\amp \left( \begin{array}{cr} 105 \amp ? \\ ? \amp 55 \\ ? \amp ? \end{array} \right)\text{.} \end{align*}

So Margo got 55 mg of Vitamin D on Wednesday. Following this line of thinking, we obtain matrix:

\begin{equation*} \left( \begin{array}{rrr} {0.2} \amp {0.1} \amp {0.5}\\ 0.2 \amp 0.3 \amp 0.1\\ 0.2 \amp 0.4 \amp 0.2 \end{array} \right)\left( \begin{array}{rrr} {100} \amp 150\\ {100} \amp 50\\ {150} \amp 100 \end{array} \right)=\left( \begin{array}{cr} 105 \amp 85 \\ 65 \amp 55 \\ 90 \amp 70 \end{array} \right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

This matrix records how much of each vitamin Margo had per day, where vitamins are rows and days are columns. So on Monday, she got 90 mg of Vitamin E, and on Wednesday, she got 85 mg of Vitamin A.

So with this illustrative example in mind, we can define matrix multiplication.

Definition 2.3.2.

For any matrix \(M\text{,}\) let \((M)_{ij}=m_{ij}\) denote the entry in the \(i\)th row and \(j\)th column. Then, given a \(m\times n\) matrix \(A\) and a \(n\times k\) matrix \(B\text{,}\) we define \(AB\) the product of \(A\) and \(B\) to be a \(m\times k\) matrix where:

\begin{equation*} (AB)_{ij}=\begin{pmatrix}\color{red}{a_{i1}} \amp \color{red}{a_{i2}} \amp \cdots \amp \color{red}{a_{in}} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}\color{blue}{b_{1j}} \\ \color{blue}{b_{2j}} \\ \vdots \\ \color{blue}{b_{nj}} \end{pmatrix} =\begin{pmatrix}\color{red}{a_{i1}}\color{blue}{b_{1j}}+\color{red}{a_{i2}}\color{blue}{b_{2j}}+\cdots+\color{red}{a_{in}}\color{blue}{b_{nj}} \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

Note that the number of columns of \(A\) and the number of rows of \(B\) must be the same. Recall Example 2.3.1, the outputs of \(B\) corresponded to the rows, and the inputs of \(A\) corresponded to it's columns, so these had to match up. This number is also the \(n\) is Definition 2.3.2.

Example 2.3.3. Matrix Products.

Let

\begin{equation*} A=\begin{pmatrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3\\ 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \end{pmatrix} , B=\begin{pmatrix}0.2 \amp 0.4 \\ 0.5 \amp 0.6 \\ 0.1 \amp 0.8 \end{pmatrix} , C=\begin{pmatrix}10 \\ 20 \\ 15 \end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Find:

  1. \(AB\text{.}\)

    \begin{align*} AB=\begin{pmatrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3\\ 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}0.2 \amp 0.4 \\ 0.5 \amp 0.6 \\ 0.1 \amp 0.8 \end{pmatrix} \amp =\amp \begin{pmatrix}1\cdot0.2+2\cdot0.5+3\cdot0.1 \amp 1\cdot0.4+2\cdot0.6+3\cdot0.8\\ 4\cdot0.2+5\cdot0.5+6\cdot0.1 \amp 4\cdot0.4+5\cdot0.6+6\cdot0.8 \end{pmatrix}\\ \amp =\amp \begin{pmatrix}1.5 \amp 4 \\ 3.9 \amp 9.4 \end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

  2. \(BA\text{.}\)

    \begin{align*} BA=\begin{pmatrix}0.2 \amp 0.4 \\ 0.5 \amp 0.6 \\ 0.1 \amp 0.8 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3\\ 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \end{pmatrix} \amp =\amp \begin{pmatrix}0.2\cdot1+ 0.4\cdot 4 \amp 0.2\cdot2+ 0.4\cdot 5 \amp 0.2\cdot3+ 0.4\cdot6\\ 0.5\cdot1+ 0.6\cdot 4 \amp 0.5\cdot2+ 0.6\cdot 5 \amp 0.5\cdot3+ 0.6\cdot6\\ 0.1\cdot1+ 0.8\cdot 4 \amp 0.1\cdot2+ 0.8\cdot 5 \amp 0.1\cdot3+ 0.8\cdot6 \end{pmatrix}\\ \amp =\amp \begin{pmatrix}1.8 \amp 2.4 \amp 3 \\ 2.9 \amp 4 \amp 5.1 \\ 3.3 \amp 4.2 \amp 5.1 \end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

  3. \(AC\text{.}\)

    \begin{align*} AC=\begin{pmatrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3\\ 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}10\\20\\15 \end{pmatrix} \amp =\amp \begin{pmatrix}1\cdot10+2\cdot20+3\cdot15 \\ 4\cdot10+5\cdot20+6\cdot15 \end{pmatrix}\\ \amp =\amp \begin{pmatrix}95 \\ 230 \end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

  4. \(CA\text{.}\) Notice that if we try to take the product \(CA\text{:}\)

    \begin{align*} CA=\begin{pmatrix}10\\20\\15 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}1 \amp 2 \amp 3\\ 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \end{pmatrix} \amp =\amp \begin{pmatrix}10\cdot1+?\cdot4 \amp 10\cdot2+?\cdot5 \amp 10\cdot3+?\cdot6 \\ 20\cdot1+?\cdot4 \amp 20\cdot2+?\cdot5 \amp 20\cdot3+?\cdot6\\15\cdot1+?\cdot4 \amp 15\cdot2+?\cdot5 \amp 15\cdot3+?\cdot6 \end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

    Since the number of columns of the first matrix do not match with the number of rows of the second matrix, the product is not defined.

  5. \(BC\text{.}\) Since \(B\) has 2 columns and \(C\) has 3 rows, these values do not match and this product is not defined.

  6. \(CB\text{.}\) Since \(C\) has 1 column and \(B\) has 2 rows, these values do not match and this product is not defined.

Something we should observe here is that, unlike products of real numbers, products of matrices are NOT commutative. In Example 2.3.3, \(AB\neq BA\text{,}\) and \(AC\) was defined whereas \(CA\) was not.

Another way matrix products deviate from real number products is that the product of matrices whose entries aren't 0 can result in a matrix whose entries are all 0.

Example 2.3.4. The zero matrix as a product of nonzero matrices.

Let

\begin{equation*} A=\begin{pmatrix}1\amp 2\\2\amp 4 \end{pmatrix} , B=\begin{pmatrix}2\amp 6\\-1\amp -3 \end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Notice that:

\begin{align*} AB=\begin{pmatrix}1\amp 2\\2\amp 4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}2\amp 6\\-1\amp -3 \end{pmatrix} \amp =\amp \begin{pmatrix}1\cdot 2+2\cdot(-1) \amp 1\cdot 6+2\cdot(-3)\\ 2\cdot 2+4\cdot(-1)\amp 2\cdot 6 +4\cdot(-3) \end{pmatrix}\\ \amp =\amp \begin{pmatrix}0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \end{pmatrix} \text{.} \end{align*}

Although neither \(A\) nor \(B\) have 0 entries, their product is an all 0 matrix. Also notice that this does not mean \(BA\) is an all 0 matrix.

\begin{align*} BA=\begin{pmatrix}2\amp 6\\-1\amp -3 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}1\amp 2\\2\amp 4 \end{pmatrix} \amp =\amp \begin{pmatrix}2\cdot 1+6\cdot2 \amp 2\cdot 2+6\cdot4\\ (-1)\cdot 1+(-3)\cdot2\amp (-1)\cdot 2 +(-3)\cdot4 \end{pmatrix}\\ \amp =\amp \begin{pmatrix}14 \amp 28 \\ -7 \amp -14 \end{pmatrix} \text{.} \end{align*}
While matrix multiplication may differ wildly from real number multiplication, we do have a notion of “multiplying by 1”.
Definition 2.3.5.

For any positive integer \(n\text{,}\) let \(I_n\) denote the \(n\times n\) matrix with 1's in the diagonal and 0 elsewhere. This is called a Identity Matrix.

So for example

\begin{equation*} I_2=\begin{pmatrix}1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}

So notice:

\begin{align*} \begin{pmatrix}1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}a \amp b \\ c \amp d \end{pmatrix}\amp = \begin{pmatrix}1\cdot a+0\cdot c \amp 1\cdot b+0\cdot d \\ 0\cdot a +1\cdot c \amp 0\cdot b +1\cdot d \end{pmatrix}\\ \amp = \begin{pmatrix}a \amp b \\ c \amp d \end{pmatrix}\\ \begin{pmatrix}a \amp b \\ c \amp d \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{pmatrix}\amp = \begin{pmatrix}a\cdot 1+b\cdot 0 \amp a\cdot 0+b\cdot 1 \\ c\cdot 1+ d\cdot 0 \amp c\cdot 0 +d\cdot 1 \end{pmatrix}\\ \amp = \begin{pmatrix}a \amp b \\ c \amp d \end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

More generally:

Remark 2.3.6.

Given a matrix \(M\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} MI_n=M=I_nM \end{equation*}

whenever these products are defined.

Subsection 2.3.2 Sums of Matrices

Matrix sums are much more straight forward than their products. Two Matrices need to have the same dimensions in order to be summed, and the sum is just the sum of the entries, so:

Definition 2.3.7.

Given two \(m\times n\) matrices \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) we define \(A+B\) the sum of \(A\) and \(B\) to be a \(m\times n\) matrix where:

\begin{equation*} (AB)_{ij}= (A_{ij}+B_{ij}) \end{equation*}

Subsection 2.3.3 Computation using Sage

As usual, this seems like it should be much easier using technology. Let's suppose \(A=\begin{pmatrix}1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 4 \end{pmatrix} , B=\begin{pmatrix}5 \amp 6 \\ 7 \amp 8 \end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) We can use Sage to find \(A+B\text{:}\)

Use:

print(A*B)
To find \(AB\) and
print(B*A)
to find \(BA\text{.}\)

Solution
\begin{align*} A+B\amp = \begin{pmatrix}6 \amp 8 \\ 10 \amp 12 \end{pmatrix}\\ AB\amp = \begin{pmatrix}19 \amp 22 \\ 43 \amp 50 \end{pmatrix}\\ BA\amp = \begin{pmatrix}23 \amp 34 \\ 31 \amp 46 \end{pmatrix} \end{align*}
Checkpoint 2.3.8.

A noodle shop has 3 locations. Their revenues and costs over June July and August are recorded in the following tables in thousands of dollars:

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c|} \textbf{Revenue} \amp \text{June} \amp \text{July} \amp \text{August}\\ \hline \text{Location 1} \amp 112 \amp 70 \amp 130\\ \hline \text{Location 2} \amp 42 \amp 50 \amp 61\\ \hline \text{Location 3} \amp 155 \amp 145 \amp 180\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c|} \textbf{Cost} \amp \text{June} \amp \text{July} \amp \text{August}\\ \hline \text{Location 1} \amp 90 \amp 85 \amp 92\\ \hline \text{Location 2} \amp 37 \amp 40 \amp 45\\ \hline \text{Location 3} \amp 120 \amp 120 \amp 130\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

Express each of these as a matrix and find a matrix representing the profit at each location.

Solution

Re-writing these as matrices, we get:

\begin{equation*} R=\begin{pmatrix} 112 \amp 70 \amp 130\\ 42 \amp 50 \amp 61\\ 55 \amp 145 \amp 180\end{pmatrix}, C= \begin{pmatrix} 90 \amp 85 \amp 92\\ 37 \amp 40 \amp 45\\ 120 \amp 120 \amp 130 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

where the row and column tells you the revenue or cost of a location in a given month. Then the profit would be:

\begin{equation*} P=R-C=\begin{pmatrix} 112 \amp 70 \amp 130\\ 42 \amp 50 \amp 61\\ 55 \amp 145 \amp 180\end{pmatrix}- \begin{pmatrix} 90 \amp 85 \amp 92\\ 37 \amp 40 \amp 45\\ 120 \amp 120 \amp 130 \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} 22 \amp -15 \amp 38\\ 5 \amp 10 \amp 16\\ 35 \amp 25 \amp 50 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Checkpoint 2.3.9.

Consider the following table recording the price of 3 different goods and the shipping cost per item.

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c|} \amp \text{Product A} \amp \text{Product B} \amp \text{Product C}\\ \hline \text{Price per item} \amp 25 \amp 30 \amp 15\\ \hline \text{Shipping cost per item} \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 2\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

Then consider this table of the quantity purchased of each good by 4 clients.

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c|c|} \amp \text{Client 1} \amp \text{Client 2} \amp \text{Client 3} \amp \text{Client 4}\\ \hline \text{Product A purchased} \amp 100 \amp 115 \amp 50\amp 45\\ \hline \text{Product B purchased} \amp 40 \amp 70 \amp 10\amp 55\\ \hline \text{Product C purchased} \amp 35 \amp 25 \amp 80\amp 50\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

Find a matrix which records the price and shipping costs incurred by each client.

Solution
We can define two matrices. Let \(A\) denote a transformation from Products to Prices:
\begin{equation*} A=\begin{pmatrix} 25 \amp 30 \amp 15\\ 2 \amp 3 \amp 2 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Then \(B\) denote a transformation from Clients to Products:
\begin{equation*} B=\begin{pmatrix} 100 \amp 115 \amp 50\amp 45\\ 40 \amp 70 \amp 10\amp 55\\ 35 \amp 25 \amp 80\amp 50\\ \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
So to form a transformation Clients to Prices, we take the product of these matrices:
\begin{equation*} AB=\begin{pmatrix} 25 \amp 30 \amp 15\\ 2 \amp 3 \amp 2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 100 \amp 115 \amp 50\amp 45\\ 40 \amp 70 \amp 10\amp 55\\ 35 \amp 25 \amp 80\amp 50\\ \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}4225 \amp 5350 \amp 2750 \amp 3525 \\ 390 \amp 490 \amp 290 \amp 355 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Thus, Client 1 spent $4225 on product and $390 on shipping. Client 2 spent $5350 on product and $490 on shipping. Client 3 spent $2750 on product and $290 on shipping. Client 4 spent $3525 on product and $355 on shipping.

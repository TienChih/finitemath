Section 5.2 Compound Interest

Almost no meaningful financial transaction in the world is computed by simple interest. The most common type of interest is compound interest which charges interest not just on your principal loan, but also on previously accrued interest as well.

Subsection 5.2.1 A first look at compound interest

So lets say you took out $100 in a loan that charged 5% monthly interest compounded monthly. That means each month, you would owe 5% more than what you owed the month before.

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|c|} \hline \text{ Month } \amp \text{ Debt } \\ \hline 0 \amp 100\\ 1\amp 100+(.05)(100)=100+5=105\\ 2\amp 105+(.05)(105)=105+5.25=110.25\\ 3 \amp 110.25+(.05)(110.25)=110.25+5.5125=115.7625\\ \vdots \amp \vdots\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

So not only is the debt increasing, but the amount that the debt increases per month, also increases. Is there an easy way to see how much we'd owe in say 10 months without computing every month in between?

Well, If I owe \(X\) dollars in any given month, then the next month I'll owe \(X+0.05X=(1.05)X\) dollars. In another month I'll owe \(1.05X+(.05)(1.05)X=(1.05)(1.05)X=X(1.05)^2\) dollars. If we apply this logic to our situation, we can see:

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|c|} \hline \text{ Month } \amp \text{ Debt } \\ \hline 0 \amp 100\\ 1\amp 100+(.05)(100)=(1.05)100\\ 2\amp (1.05)100+(.05)((1.05)100)=(1.05)(1.05)100=(1.05)^2 100\\ 3 \amp (1.05)^2100+(.05)((1.05)^2100)=(1.05)(1.05)^2 100=(1.05)^3 100\\ \vdots \amp \vdots\\ n \amp (1.05)^n100\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

So in 10 months, we'd owe \(100\cdot (1.05)^{10}\approx \$162.89\text{.}\) In general, this forms an exponential function:

In general, if the interest rate per time unit is \(i\text{,}\) then after \(n\) time units:

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|c|} \hline \text{ Time Unit } \amp \text{ Debt } \\ \hline 0 \amp P\\ 1\amp P+iP=(1+i)P\\ 2\amp (1+i)P+i(1+i)P=(1+i)^2P\\ 3 \amp (1+i)^2P+i(1+i)^2P=(1+i)^3P\\ \vdots \amp \vdots\\ n \amp (1+i)^nP\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}

So we have the general equation

\begin{align*} A\amp =P(1+i)^n,\\ I\amp =P(1+i)^n-P. \end{align*}

Where \(A\) is the accumulated value, \(P\) is the value of the principal, \(i\) is the interest rate, \(n\) is the number of time units, and \(I\) is the total value of the interest.

But looking at \(A =P(1+i)^n\text{,}\) if one knew any 3 of these values, in principle one should be able to find the 4th.

Example 5.2.1. Future Value with compound interest.

Suppose that I make an investment $500 at 8% annual interest. How much will this investment be worth in 10 years?

Solution

So we note that:

\begin{align*} P\amp = 500\\ i\amp = 0.08\\ n\amp = 10 \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{equation*} A=500(1+.08)^{10}\approx 10794.62. \end{equation*}

Example 5.2.2. Present Value with compound interest.

Suppose that I take out a loan at 4% a year, and after 5 years, I owe \(\$7,000\text{.}\) How much was the initial loan?

Solution

So we note that:

\begin{align*} A\amp = 7000\\ i\amp = 0.04\\ n\amp = 5 \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{align*} 7000\amp =P(1+.04)^{5}\\ P\amp =\frac{7000}{(1.04)^5}\approx 5753.49. \end{align*}

So the initial value of the loan is of \(\$5753.49\text{.}\)

Example 5.2.3. Interest Rate with compound interest.

Suppose that I purchase a property for \(\$200,000\) and in 30 years it is worth \(\$350,000\text{.}\) What is the annual interest rate of the value of this property?

Solution

So we note that:

\begin{align*} A\amp = 350000\\ P\amp = 200000\\ n\amp = 30 \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{align*} 350000\amp =200000(1+i)^{30}\\ \frac{350000}{200000}\amp =(1+i)^{30}\\ (1+i)^{30}\amp= 1.75\\ (1+i)\amp= (1.75)^{1/30}\approx 1.018829\\ i\amp \approx 0.018829 \end{align*}

So the interest is about \(1.8829\%\text{.}\)

Example 5.2.4. Time of Investment with compound interest.

Suppose that I invested \(\$50,000\) at 7% annual interest. How long would it take for this investment to be worth \(\$100,000\text{.}\)

Solution

So we note that:

\begin{align*} A\amp = 100000\\ P\amp = 50000\\ i\amp = 0.07 \end{align*}

If one is familiar with the properties of logs and natural logs:

\begin{align*} 100000\amp =50000(1.07)^{n}\\ 2\amp =(1.07)^{n}\\ \ln(2)\amp= \ln(1.07^n)\\ \ln(2)\amp= n\ln(1.07)\\ n\amp=\frac{\ln(2)}{\ln(1.07)} \approx 10.24477 \end{align*}

So about \(10.24477\) years.

Otherwise, since we know \(P, i\) we can simply plot the graph and see when it reaches 50000: So about 10.245 years.

Subsection 5.2.2 AN ISSUE OF EXTREME ANNOYANCE

In the above examples, we work with interest rates described in terms of the time units in which they are discussed. However, financial companies have devised a concept that obfuscates this principle via an artificial notion of “compounding periods”.

Suppose you have a debt of $100 that accrues 10% annual interest. With no other given information, we would say that the amount you owe in \(10\) years would be

\begin{equation*} A=100(1.1)^{10}\approx \$259.37 \end{equation*}

right? But what a financial institution will do is say that the debt is “compounded quarterly” i.e. 4 times a year. Thus instead of 10% interest per year, it will now be \(10\%/4=2.5\%\) interest per quarter. How much will you owe in 10 years? Well, 10 years is 40 quarters so:

\begin{equation*} A=100(1.025)^{40}\approx 268.51 \end{equation*}

Similarly, if the compounding is done monthly, then this is 12 times a year, and each month you'd be charged at a rate of \(\frac{10\%}{12}\text{.}\) So in 10 years or 120 months, you would owe:

\begin{equation*} A=100\left(1+\frac{0.1}{12}\right)^{120}\approx 270.70 \end{equation*}

My belief is this accomplishes 2 things:

  1. By making the issue more complicated, it makes it more difficult for consumers to figure out how much they are actually going to be charged in interest.

  2. By increasing compounding periods, you can effectively charge more interest, see how much the debt's grow in the above scenarios?

In general if you are charged an annual interest rate of \(r\) compounded \(m\) times a year for \(t\) years. You are really getting charged

\begin{equation*} i=\frac{r}{m} \end{equation*}

interest rate per time unit, and the number of time units will be

\begin{equation*} n=mt\text{.} \end{equation*}

How much yearly interest is there really? It's how much interest you end up accruing in a year: Let \(r_E\) be the effective annual interest:

\begin{align*} P(1+r_E)\amp = P(1+r/m)^{m}\\ 1+r_E\amp = (1+r/m)^{m}\\ r_E\amp = (1+r/m)^{m}-1 \end{align*}

So 10% interest compounded quarterly is effectively:

\begin{equation*} r_E=(1+0.1/4)^4-1\approx0.1038 \end{equation*}

or 10.38% annual interest. Whereas 10% compounded monthly is

\begin{equation*} r_E=(1+0.1/12)^{12}-1\approx 0.1047 \end{equation*}

or 10.47% annual interest.

This artifical interest rate is often referred to as APR or Annual Percentage Rate.

Checkpoint 5.2.5. Future Value.

Suppose that I take out a loan for $500 at 12% annual interest charged biannually. How much will I owe in 10 years?

Solution

So we note that:

\begin{align*} P\amp = 500\ \text{ the initial loan }\\ i\amp = 0.12/2=0.06 \ \text{ the interest rate per half-year }\\ n\amp = 10\cdot 2=20 \ \text{ the number of half-years } \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{equation*} A=500(1+.06)^{20}\approx 1603.57\text{.} \end{equation*}

Checkpoint 5.2.6. Size of Initial Investment.

Suppose that I want to invest some money at 8% annual interest compounded annually and after 20 years I want $2000. How much should I invest?

Solution

So we note that:

\begin{align*} A\amp = 2000, \ \text{ the future value }\\ i\amp = 0.08, \ \text{ the actual interest rate }\\ n\amp = 20, \ \text{ number of years } \end{align*}

(Since interest is compounded annually, 0.08 is the actual annual interest rate.)

So we are solving:

\begin{align*} 2000\amp =\amp P(1.08)^{20}\\ P\amp =\amp \frac{2000}{1.08^{20}}\approx429.10 \end{align*}

Checkpoint 5.2.7. Interest Rates.

Suppose that I want to invest $500 and in 10 years I want this to grow to $2000. What sort of interest would make that possible?

Solution

So we have:

\begin{align*} P\amp = 500\\ A\amp = 2000\\ n\amp = 10 \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{align*} 2000\amp = 500(1+i)^{10}\\ 4\amp = (1+i)^{10}\\ 4^{1/10}\amp=1+i\\ i\amp = 4^{1/10}-1\approx0.1487 \end{align*}

or 14.87% annual interest.

Checkpoint 5.2.8. Length of a Loan.

Suppose that I borrow $1000 at 24% annual interest compounded monthly. How long until I owe $20,000?

Solution

So we have:

\begin{align*} P\amp = 1000\\ A\amp = 20000\\ i\amp = 0.24.12=0.02 \end{align*}

So we are solving:

\begin{align*} 20000\amp = 1000(1.02)^n\\ 20\amp = (1.02)^n\\ \ln(20)\amp = n\ln(1.02)\\ n\amp = \ln(20)/\ln(1.02)\approx151.28 \end{align*}

So in about 151-152 months, I will owe 20,000 dollars.

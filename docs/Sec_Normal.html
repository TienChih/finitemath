Section 10.3 Normal Distributions

In this section, we study one of the most ubiquitous and fundamental probability distributions in nature and mathematics: the normal distribution. We may know this as the standard “bell curve”.

Subsection 10.3.1 The standard Normal Curve

Definition 10.3.1.

The standard normal curve is the curve generated by

\begin{equation*} y=\frac{e^{-x^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}. \end{equation*}

This may be seen below.

We observe some things about this curve:

  • The peak of this curve is at \(x=0\)
  • The curve is symmetric, it's behavior on the one side mirrors that of the other.
  • It's values are largest the closer the \(x\) values are to zero.
  • The bulk of the area under the curve falls between -3 and 3, and otherwise tapers off as \(x\) approaches \(\infty, -\infty\text{.}\)

These are all things we would intuitively associate with a bell curve.

Remark 10.3.2.

The standard normal curve is the probability distribution of the standard normal distribution, the normal distribution with mean 0 and standard deviation 1. For continuous distributions such as the normal distribution, we can have valid values that are any real number, as opposed to a finite or countable list. Consequently we compute probabilities by finding areas under this curve.

We use \(Z\) to denote the standard normal random variable, the probability that \(Z\) is between \(a\) and \(b\) is the area under the normal curve between \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)

Finding these values is generally done through machine-assisted computations. Many texts use a \(z\)-score table presentation where values of \(z\) and associated probabilities are listed. However, these are just values computed by machines listed on paper. This made sense in a day and age were computing power wasn't universal. If you're reading this, this is clearly no longer the case.

So if we wanted to find say \(P(-1.2\lt Z\leq 2)\) this could be done as follows: This is the standard curve so the mean \(\mu=0\) and the standard deviation \(\sigma=1\text{.}\) The bounds of our region are \(a=-1.2, b=2\) respectively. So \(P(-1.2\lt z\leq 2)\approx0.8622.\)

By typing “infinity” into the bounds, we can also find the areas of unbounded regions. If we wanted to find say \(P(z>1.5)\text{,}\) this is equivalent to \(P(1.5\lt z\lt \infty)\text{.}\) Thus \(P(Z>1.5)\approx 0.0668\text{.}\)

Remark 10.3.3. Some things to keep in mind.

Here are some quick facts and heuristics about the normal curve to give you an intuitive sense on how it behaves.

  • Each half is 50% of the curve, so have greater than the mean, half less than the mean.
  • For any tail of the distribution, the corresponding tail on the other side has the same area.
  • Roughly 67% of the curve lies within \(\pm 1\) standard deviations from the mean, 95% within \(\pm 2\) standard deviations from the mean, and 99.7% within \(\pm 3\) standard deviations from the mean.

Subsection 10.3.2 General Normal Distributions

The fact is, most things we care about in the real world don't happen to have mean 0 and standard deviation 1. So we introduce the notion of \(general normal distributions\text{.}\) Things that are generally normally distributed may have any mean and standard deviation, but what makes them normally distributed is that they have the same fundamental shape as the standard normal, a bell curved symmetric shape. In the standard normal curve, the standard deviation is 1, so the units of \(z\) are standard deviations. We can translate values from any \(X\) normally distributed random variable to the standard distribution through \(z\)-scores.

Definition 10.3.5.

Given a normally distributed variable \(X\) with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\text{,}\) the \(z\)-score of a value \(x\) of \(X\) is the number of standard deviations \(x\) is above or below the mean. This is computed

\begin{equation*} z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}. \end{equation*}

So if we had a normally distributed variable \(X\) with \(\mu=10, \sigma=3\text{,}\) if we wanted to find \(P(8\lt X\lt 11)\text{,}\) we could turn this into a standard normal problem.

So the \(z\)-score of \(8\) would be

\begin{equation*} z=\frac{8-10}{3}=-\frac{2}{3}. \end{equation*}

Similarly, the \(z\)-score of \(11\) would be

\begin{equation*} z=\frac{11-10}{3}=\frac{1}{3}. \end{equation*}

So

\begin{equation*} P(8\lt X\lt 11)=P(-\frac{2}{3}\lt Z\lt \frac{1}{3}). \end{equation*}

See below: Play around with shifting \(m,s,a,b\) to see this changing relationship between \(X\) and \(Z\text{!}\)

We can compute this problem either way, through \(P(-\frac{2}{3}\lt Z\lt \frac{1}{3})\approx 0.3781\text{:}\) Or more directly \(P(8\lt X\lt 11)\approx 0.3781\text{:}\) Scrolling down the left side, we can see the \(z\)-scores are computed automatically.

Example 10.3.6.

Suppose Ahi tuna weights are normally distributed with mean 120 lbs and standard deviation 25 lb's. Kyle the fisherman goes out fishing.

  1. What is the probability that a tuna he catches weighs over 150 lbs?
  2. What is the probability that a tuna he catches weighs between 50 and 100 lbs?
  3. Out of all the tuna Kyle catches, find a cutoff weight for the smallest 15%
  4. Find a \(k\) so that 75% of the tuna caught falls between \(120-k\) and \(120+k\) pounds.
Solution 1

\(P(X>150)\approx 0.1151\text{.}\)

Solution 2

\(P(50\leq X\leq 100)\approx 0.2093\text{.}\)

Solution 3

15% of Tuna weigh less than 94.0892.

Solution 4

75% of Tuna weigh between 91.2412 and 148.7587 pounds. So \(k\approx 148.7587-120=28.7587\) or \(k\approx 120-91.2412=28.7587\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 10.3.7.

Marathon running times are normally distributed with mean 4.5 hours and standard deviation 1 hour.

  1. What proportion of runners would take over 6 hours?
  2. Suppose a special award is given to the top 10% fastest runners. What would be the predicted cutoff for this time?
Solution 1

\(P(X>6)\approx 0.0668\text{.}\)

Solution 2

10% of runners would finish in 3.2184 hours or less. That is 3 hours and about 13 minutes.

Checkpoint 10.3.8.

A business's weekly profit is normally distributed with mean \(\$2000\) and standard deviation \(\$1200\text{.}\)

  1. What percentage of weeks are profitable?
  2. Find the bounds of the 50% middle-most profitable weeks.
Solution 1

\(P(X>0)\approx 0.7977\text{.}\)

Solution 2

The middle 50% of weeks range from \(\$109.61\) to \(\$1809.39\) in profit.

Checkpoint 10.3.9.

A machine deposits milk into a bottle, the output is normal with mean 32 oz and standard deviation 0.65 oz.

  1. An acceptable range for a vendor is 31-34oz. What percentage of bottles satisfy this?
  2. What is the cutoff for the 10% smallest bottles?
Solution 1

\(P(31\lt x\lt 34)\approx 0.9370\text{.}\)

Solution 2

The bottom 10% is any bottle smaller than about 31.167 oz.

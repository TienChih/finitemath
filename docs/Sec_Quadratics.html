Section 6.2 Quadratic Functions

Subsection 6.2.1 Form of Quadratic functions

Definition 6.2.1.

A function \(q(x)\) is a quadratic function if it may be written as:

\begin{equation*} q(x)=ax^2+bx+c\text{.} \end{equation*}

where \(a\neq0\text{.}\)

The classic example is the standard parabola, \(y=x^2\) or a quadratic with \(a=1.\) \(b,c=0\text{.}\) We should be familiar with some of the properties of this shape, it's symmetric on both sides and it goes to positive infinity in both directions (as \(x^2\) cannot be negative).

It would be nice if we could show that all quadratics had the same essential shape. To do this, we must first show that all quadratics also have the form

\begin{equation*} q(x)=a(x-h)^2+k\text{.} \end{equation*}

To see this:

\begin{align*} q(x)\amp = ax^2+bx+c\\ \amp = a\left(x^2+\frac{b}{a}x\right)+c\\ \amp = a\left(x^2+2\frac{b}{2a}x+\frac{b^2}{4a^2}\right)+c-\frac{b^2}{4a}\\ \amp = a\left(x+\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2+c-\frac{b^2}{4a} \end{align*}

So by letting \(h=-\frac{b}{2a}\) and \(k=c-\frac{b^2}{4a}\text{,}\) we have our form.

An interesting byproduct of this is that if we were to solve \(ax^2+bx+c=0\text{,}\) we would get:

\begin{align*} ax^2+bx+c\amp = 0\\ a(x+\frac{b}{2a})^2+c-\frac{b^2}{4a}\amp = 0\\ a(x+\frac{b}{2a})^2\amp=\frac{b^2}{4a}-c\\ a(x+\frac{b}{2a})^2\amp=\frac{b^2-4ac}{4a}\\ (x+\frac{b}{2a})^2\amp=\frac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}\\ x+\frac{b}{2a}\amp=\frac{\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\\ x\amp =\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \end{align*}

Which is the quadratic formula.

Subsection 6.2.2 Parabolas

Now that we have this form \(a(x-h)^2+k\text{,}\) consider how \(a,h\text{,}\) and \(k\) affect the shape of the graph:

Sliding \(k\) shifts the parabola up and down. We can think of this as adding to or detracting from the value of \(x^2\text{.}\) Sliding \(h\) shifts the graph left and right. We can think of this as slowing down or speeding up \(x^2\) by \(h\) units. \(a\) stretches or squashes \(x^2\text{,}\) or if \(a\) is negative, also reflects this across the \(x\) axis. But all of these transformations preserve the fundamental shape of a parabola, so all quadratic functions have this shape.

Note that since \(ax^2+bx+c= a(x-h)^2+k\) is a shift of the standard parabola by \(h\) and \(k\text{,}\) we have that the vertex of \(y=ax^2+bx+c\) the maximum or minimum value, is the point \((h,k)=(-\frac{b}{2a}, c-\frac{b^2}{4a})\text{.}\)

A less computationally intense way of finding the vertex is to see where the roots of the quadratic are, that is, where the parabola has \(x\)-intercepts. Due to the symmetry of a parabola, the vertex must occur halfway in between.

If we consider the parabola \(y=x(20-x)\) the roots occur at \(x=0, 10\) , the the \(x\) value of the vertex is halfway between these points at \(x=10\text{,}\) and the \(y\) value is \((10,(20-10)=100\text{.}\) One could rewrite \(y=-x^2+20x+0\) and find the vertex that way as well.

Example 6.2.2.

Suppose that in an apartment complex of 80 units, if the rent is \(\$800 \) a month, all the units will be filled. For every \(\$25\) we increase the rent there will be an additional vacancy. What rent maximizes the monthly revenue?

Solution

We note that the monthly revenue generated by the apartment complex is \(O\cdot T\) where \(O\) is the occupancy and \(T\) is the rent (the rent per unit, times the number of units rented).

We note that if \(x\) is the amount in dollars above \(\$800\) that you charge, then \(O=80-\frac{x}{25}\text{,}\) that is, occupancy starts at 80 and decreases one per \(\$25\text{.}\) On the other hand, the rent \(T=800+x\text{.}\) So \(R(x)=(80-\frac{x}{25})(800+x)=-\frac{1}{25}x^2+48x+64000\text{.}\)

So our vertex occurs at \(h=-\frac{48}{2\cdot \frac{-1}{25}}=600\) and \(k=64000-\frac{48^2}{4\cdot \frac{-1}{25}}=78400\text{.}\)

Since this \(h=600\) is how much the rent is increased, the revenue is maximized when the rent is \(800+600=\$1400\) a month, with an occupancy of \(80-\frac{600}{25}=56\) units, for a total revenue of \(\$78400\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 6.2.3.

Suppose that you were given 200 ft of fencing to fence in a rectangular area. What is the largest are you could fence in and what would it's dimensions be?

Solution

We note that a rectangle has length sides and two width sides. If \(\ell \) is the length of the length sides and \(w\) the width side, we obtain:

\begin{equation*} 2\ell+2w=200 \end{equation*}

or \(\ell+w=100\text{.}\) We also have that the area of a rectangle is \(A=\ell w\text{.}\) Thus:

\begin{align*} A\amp = \ell w\\ A\amp = (100-w)w\\ A\amp = -w^2+100w. \end{align*}

Thus the fenced region has area 0 when \(w=0,100\) the vertex must occur halfway in between at \(w=50, \ell=100-50=50, A=50\cdot 50=2500\) square feet.

Checkpoint 6.2.4.

A company sells widgets for \(\$50\) each. Unfortunately, no one wants to buy them. The discover that for each dollar they lower the price, 5 more people are willing to buy a widget. The marginal cost of producing a widget is \(\$10\) with fixed cost \(\$300\text{.}\) How should the widget be priced to maximize profit? What is this profit, and how many widgets would be sold?

Solution

Let's let\(x\) denote the value by which we decrease the price of a widget from \(\$50\text{.}\) We'll note that the quantity sold is \(q=5x\text{.}\) From this we deduce:

  • The revenue is the price of a widget, times the number sold. So \(R(x)=(50-x)q=(50-x)5x\text{.}\)
  • The cost is the fixed cost of \(\$300\text{,}\) plus the marginal cost \(\$10\) times the number sold. So \(RCx)=300+10q=300+10(5x)=300+50x\text{.}\)
  • The profit is revenue minus cost. So \(P(x)=R(x)-C(x)=(50-x)5x-(300+50x)=-5x^2+200x-300\text{.}\)

Which by whatever method you most prefer, has vertex \((20,1700)\text{.}\) So the profit is maximized when the price is reduced by \(\$20\) or when the price is \(p=\$30\text{.}\) The profit here would be \(\$1700\) with \(20\cdot5=100\) widgets sold.

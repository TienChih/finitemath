So we now arrive at the interesting version of this problem, but it's fundamentally not different from the above version. So let's see:

  • There are \(C(n,k)\) ways one could select \(k\) out of the \(n\) exams to receive an A.
  • Given any of those, the chance she gets A's on those \(k\) exams is \(p\) per exam, so to get A's on all of them has prob \(p^k\text{.}\)
  • Then for the remaining \(n-k\) exams, each has a \(1-p\) chance of not getting an A. So the chance that those exams don't receive an A is \((1-p)^{n-k}\text{.}\)

Putting these together, the probability that she receives those $k$ A's is:

\begin{equation*} C(n,k) p^k (1-p)^{n-k}. \end{equation*}

Of course, this all extends beyond exam grades and A's.
